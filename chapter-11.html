Part One: The Making of a Young Man  ·  Chapter Eleven

Running the Whole Mission

D
Dee Stevens Buffalo & Rochester, New York  ·  1971
❧

By the time I arrived in Buffalo as a zone leader, I had been on my mission for over a year. I had tracted in the snow, taught in living rooms, watched people receive witnesses from the Holy Ghost, and watched others walk away from the door of something good. I had served under strong companions and learned from all of them. I thought I had a reasonable sense of what missionary work looked like when it was going well.

The Buffalo zone showed me what it looked like when it was not going well at all.

My companion was Elder Larry Nilson. We inherited a zone that had been sitting at the bottom of the mission in baptisms for over a year — seventh out of seven zones, dead last, and had been there long enough that people had stopped expecting anything different. The elders were going through the motions. The district leaders were managing rather than leading. The energy was gone.

Our zone's rank 7th Dead last in the mission. Had been there for over a year.
Zones in the mission 7 130 missionaries total. Rochester zone consistently led.
Districts we managed 4 Spread across the Buffalo area, each with their own district leader.
Time we had 1 month To try something new and see if it made any difference at all.

Elder Nilson and I looked at what we had and tried to understand why it was so flat. The elders weren't lazy. The district leaders weren't incompetent. Something else was wrong — something in the structure, the energy, the way the young missionaries were relating to the work and to each other.

We decided to try something that had not been tried before in our mission.

· · ·
The Nilson-Stevens Plan

We would spend a week in each of the four districts — working alongside the elders personally, giving guidance to the district leaders, and then on Saturday we would gather at a church for a district workshop. We drilled the discussions. We had scripture contests. We trained hard in the morning, ate lunch together provided by ward members, and then spent the afternoon playing basketball or football. The brains were exercised. The Spirit was exercised. Their bodies got some well-deserved exercise. Then we moved to the next district and did it again.

The mission president had banned physical sports after too many elders had been injured playing them. We knew this. We proceeded anyway, reasoning — or hoping — that what we were observing in the zone was exactly the kind of backfire that came from keeping young men from playing hard. They needed an outlet. They needed to laugh together. They needed to feel like people, not just workers.

After a month we had gone through all four districts. The reports coming in had a different energy. The missionaries were challenging their investigators. People were accepting baptism. The numbers were moving.

· · ·

Then the president called.

He asked me, directly and without preamble, if I was morally clean. I told him I was. He said he wanted Elder Nilson and me in his office the following morning.

We spent the rest of that day trying to figure out what we had done wrong. We had broken the sports rule. We had neglected our own area to work in the districts. We had done things outside the prescribed structure. We drove toward Rochester the next morning mostly in silence, preparing ourselves for whatever was coming.

The New York Thruway · Somewhere between Buffalo and Rochester
"I became aware that I had fallen asleep at the wheel and had begun to drift."
The shock woke me right up. I realized the Lord wanted to keep Elder Nilson alive — and was not going to let us crash before we found out what we were driving toward.

We arrived at the mission office and sat across from President Stephenson. The three assistants were also in the room. I expected a rebuke. I had prepared myself for one.

Mission President Stephenson's office · Rochester, New York
"I want to know how in a month's time you brought the Buffalo zone — which has been the basement of the mission for the last year — up to give Rochester some very serious competition."
— President Stephenson

We carefully explained the workshop plan. We explained that it seemed to fuse new life into the young elders, and that honestly, we were as surprised as he was at the quick results. We expressed our opinion — respectfully, carefully — that keeping young men from playing hard would backfire over time. That they needed an outlet alongside the serious work of their calling. That the two things were not in conflict.

He listened. He turned to two of his assistants and told them to get the details of our workshops in writing and distribute them to the other zone leaders. The Nilson-Stevens plan was to become the new Cumorah Mission plan.

"The third assistant was going home. I was transferred into the mission office to take his place. We had gone from the bottom of the mission to running it — in one month, with a plan we had made up as we went."

· · ·

I want to say something about what I learned in Buffalo, because I think it applies far beyond missionary work.

The zone was not failing because the elders were bad. It was failing because nobody had given them a reason to believe it could be different. Nobody had sat with them, worked beside them, eaten lunch with them, played basketball with them on a Saturday afternoon and then asked them to give everything they had on Monday morning. Leadership at a distance produces results at a distance. Leadership in the room produces something else entirely.

I have tried to remember that in every leadership role I have held since. As a Young Men's president. As a store director. As a teacher. As a father. The people under your care do not need more management. They need someone willing to show up in their district, work alongside them, eat lunch with them, and let them beat you at basketball occasionally.

Then ask everything of them. They will give it.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa inherited the worst-performing zone in his entire mission and in one month turned it into the second best. The plan he used — train hard, eat together, play hard, then get back to work — became the official plan for the whole mission. The president called him into the office expecting to hand out a rebuke and ended up promoting him instead. Grandpa almost fell asleep driving there. The Lord kept him on the road long enough to find out what was waiting at the other end.
