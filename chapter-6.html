Part One · The Making of a Young Man Chapter Six

“It’s True! Why Didn’t You Tell Me Before?”

Rochester, New York · 1970

Before I tell you about Sister Raina, I want to tell you what it actually feels like to teach someone the gospel — because until you have done it, the words don't quite capture it.

You sit in someone's living room, usually in the evening, usually on furniture that has seen better days, usually with a companion who is also nineteen and also hoping this goes well. You have your discussions memorized. You have your scriptures. You have your testimony, which is the most real thing you own.

And you talk. You teach. You answer questions. You testify. And most of the time the person across from you is polite and noncommittal and you leave not quite sure what you accomplished.

But every so often — not often, but often enough to keep you going — something happens in a room that you cannot manufacture and cannot predict and cannot take credit for. The Spirit of the Lord enters, and you feel it, and the person across from you feels it, and something that was closed begins to open.

I had been on my mission for a few months when I witnessed this for the first time. I have never forgotten it. I do not think I am capable of forgetting it.

· · ·

Sister Raina had been meeting with Elder Gray before I arrived in Rochester. She liked the elders and she continued to receive the lessons — she was warm and engaged and genuinely curious — but something seemed to be keeping her from crossing over. From believing. She would get close and then pull back, or seem to plateau at a certain point of interest without moving further. Elder Gray had been patient with her for weeks.

He knew his scriptures, and through the guidance of the Holy Ghost, he developed a lesson he believed might finally reach her. I watched him prepare it with care — the kind of care a craftsman gives to a piece of work he knows matters.

What Elder Gray had her read

The sticks of Joseph and Judah — Ezekiel 37:15–17. Two sticks written upon, to be joined into one in the Lord's hand. One stick for the Bible. One stick for the Book of Mormon. Two witnesses from two branches of Israel, bound together as one record of the Savior.

As the lesson moved along I could feel the Spirit settling into the room the way it does — not dramatically, but unmistakably, like a change in air pressure before a storm. Sister Raina was following Elder Gray with complete attention. She had read several pages from the Book of Mormon by then and she could see where he was heading. Her face was serious in the way that faces get serious when something important is being decided.

Then Elder Gray had her read the passage about the sticks. She read it slowly. He testified to her — humbly, quietly, without performance — that the Book of Mormon was true scripture. That Joseph Smith was a prophet. That God had restored His church to the earth.

And then it happened.

Rochester, New York · 1970

“It’s true! It’s true!”

“Why didn’t you tell me this before?”

Her eyes lit up. That is the only way I know to say it. Something came on in her face — a light, a recognition, an expression of sublime happiness that I had never seen on a person's face before and that I recognized immediately as something I had no part in producing.

She wept openly and without embarrassment. She hugged Elder Gray until his face went red.

I sat there in that living room and did not move for a moment. I was nineteen years old and I had just watched a human being receive a witness from the Holy Ghost. I had watched the moment a soul that had been searching finally found what it was looking for.

She was baptized shortly afterward.

· · ·

I have thought about Sister Raina's question many times over the years. Why didn't you tell me this before?

On the surface it was addressed to Elder Gray — a gentle reproach from a woman who felt she had been circling the truth for weeks without quite being shown it directly. But the more I have thought about it, the more I believe the question was bigger than that. It was the question of someone who has just understood something they realize they have always needed to understand, and who cannot quite believe it took this long to arrive.

"She wasn't asking why we hadn't told her sooner. She was asking why no one in her whole life had ever told her this. Why the world had withheld from her something this true."

I have heard echoes of that question in other rooms, in other years, from other people. The truck driver in Rochester who found a Book of Mormon in an Ohio laundromat and drove through the night because he needed answers right now. The father in Saratoga Springs who ran out to challenge the Jehovah's Witnesses on his sidewalk because he had read something true and could not contain it. The eighty-two-year-old woman in a rocking chair who had been dreaming about two young men in suits and needed to know what they were going to teach her.

The question underneath all of it is the same: why did it take so long to find this?

I don't have a complete answer to that. What I have is the witness of a lifetime of watching it happen — watching people encounter a truth they recognize in their bones, sometimes after decades of searching, sometimes after a single evening in a living room in Rochester. The timing is not ours to arrange. Our job is simply to be in the room, prepared, when the Spirit decides it is time.

Elder Gray was prepared. I watched him be prepared. I watched what preparation made possible.

Sister Raina's face — that light, that expression of sublime happiness — is the picture I reach for when the work is hard and the doors are closed and the days are cold. That is what we are out there for. That is what it looks like when it works.

I saw it once, in a living room in Rochester, when I was nineteen years old. It was enough to last me a lifetime.

A Note for the Grandkids

This was the first time Grandpa watched someone receive a witness from the Holy Ghost — the moment a woman named Sister Raina understood something she had been circling for weeks, and cried out that it was true. He was 19 years old. He has never forgotten her face. The question she asked — why didn't you tell me this before? — is one he has been thinking about ever since.
