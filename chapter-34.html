Part Six: Building a Life  ·  Chapter Thirty-Four

The Floor

D
Dee Stevens Orem, Utah  ·  2024–2025
✦

The ankle surgery went well enough. What came next did not.

I was recovering at Daniel and Jolyn's home when I broke my leg — the same one that had been fused. It was the kind of setback that arrives without warning and requires a complete recalibration of what the next several months will look like. I ended up in a care center in Orem for the first half of 2025, working through physical therapy, relearning how to move through the world on a body that was increasingly reluctant to cooperate.

I want to be honest about what that time was like, because I think honesty is more useful than reassurance. It was hard. I had been a man who moved through the world — who drove himself places, who managed grocery stores and coached boys and hauled equipment and stood at a chalkboard for nine years. The care center was a different kind of life, and I accepted it about as gracefully as you would expect from a person who spent thirty years in grocery management and nine years in a middle school classroom.

But the Lord arranges the thing that is needed, not the thing that is convenient, and what was arranged in those months was good.

· · ·

Linda had been working a four-hour shift at the Orem Temple each Wednesday. That summer, once I had recovered enough to move around, I found the boldness to ask if she would take me with her. She said yes. So for nearly every week of that summer I sat in the Orem Temple for four hours doing ordinance work while Linda worked her shift.

Orem Utah Temple  ·  Summer 2025
Four hours each Wednesday. Linda at her shift. Dee at the font.
The words of the ordinances began to enculturate into his soul, he said — to become part of who he was.
The sealers and secretaries in the sealing office remembered him. They greeted him by name.
He looked forward to it every week. It was, he said later, exactly what he needed.

There is something that happens when you spend regular time in the temple that I cannot fully explain and will not try to. I can say that the words of the ordinances, heard week after week, stopped being words I was processing and became words I was living inside. The house of the Lord had always been important to me. That summer it became something else — a anchor, a weekly reminder of what all of this is for, sitting in a quiet room with Linda nearby and the names of the dead in my hands.

· · ·

By December of 2025 I had begun to feel genuinely better. I was leaving the walker behind in certain rooms, moving through the house by holding onto furniture and cabinets — carefully, but moving. I had started to believe the worst of the recovery was behind me.

Then one Friday night I fell asleep in my recliner.

By the time I woke it was six in the morning. I thought I would go upstairs to my room and sleep a few more hours. A reasonable plan. I made my way from the recliner to the base of the stairs, holding the back of the couch, and discovered my legs would not lift high enough to climb the first step. I sat down on the bottom stair to think through my options.

I woke up an hour later still sitting there, having fallen asleep again on the step.

Orem, Utah  ·  December 2025
Legs not strong enough to stand. Tried crawling to the bathroom to pull himself up — couldn't.
Tried crawling to the refrigerator. He was thirsty. He could not reach the water.
He moved toward the living room. He lay down on the floor.
Sunday morning, 10:30 AM. His friend Cobe Peay came to pick him up for church.

Cobe found me on the floor. I will not elaborate on the details of what that looked like except to say that I had been there for many hours and the situation was what you would expect. He was able to reach Travis and Daniel, and I found myself at the hospital shortly after, diagnosed with pneumonia.

I have had pneumonia on my mind since the year 2000, when I received a transplanted kidney and knew that two or three members of my extended family had died from post-transplant pneumonia within weeks of their surgeries. I had received a priesthood blessing in our home in Pleasant Grove that year — two elderly high priests laying their hands on my head — and I had gotten up from the chair and never looked back. Twenty-five years later here I was again. The old fear was familiar. The borrowed kidney was still working. The Lord had kept His word before. I had reason to believe He would again.

What it meant that Cobe came

He came because it was Sunday morning and Dee had said he would be ready. He came because that is the kind of man he is. He came because the Lord arranges, as He always has, for the right person to be in the right place at the right time — not dramatically, not with fanfare, but dependably, in the form of a friend with a car and a reason to knock on a door.

Dee has been found his whole life by people like this. The nurse who noticed the soaking pillow. The state official who looked at the bruises. The neighbor who was a policeman. Cobe Peay on a Sunday morning. The Lord does not always send angels. Sometimes He sends friends.

· · ·

I am writing this from the other side of that hospital stay, which means I am still here, which continues to be the main thing worth reporting.

Seventy-four years old. A borrowed kidney in its twenty-fifth year. A fused ankle. A broken leg healed. Pneumonia treated. A body that has been through more than I would have chosen for it, and that has kept going anyway, which is all any of us can do.

"I have been found on floors before — not always literal ones. The Lord has always sent someone. I have learned to stop being surprised by this and start being grateful for it while it is happening."

Linda came. My sons came. The ward came. The meals came back to the porch the way they had come after Kathy died — quietly, without ceremony, the way service arrives from people who have decided you are worth the trouble.

I have been worth the trouble to a great many people over the years. I do not fully understand why. I know I have tried to hoe to the end of the row — in every calling, every store, every classroom, every family history project, every year of this long and unruly life. I know I have tried to be present for the people who needed someone present. I know I have tried to pay an honest tithe and keep an honest faith and say an honest prayer when things were dark.

Whether that is enough to account for all the people who have shown up for me, I cannot say. What I can say is that they showed up. They keep showing up. And I do not think that is coincidence. I think that is a God who has been paying attention since before I was born, arranging the pieces for a life He had something in mind for — keeping me available, as it turns out, for a little longer yet.

I do not know exactly how much longer. I have stopped trying to calculate it. The kidney has outlasted every prediction. My legs keep finding a way. The pneumonia was treated and I came home. The floor was not where the story ended.

It rarely is.
