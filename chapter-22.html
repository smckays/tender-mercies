A Life of Tender Mercies  ·  Dee Stevens
Part Three: Kathy  ·  Chapter Twenty-Two

Adventures in Arizona

D
Dee Stevens Tucson & Sierra Vista, Arizona  ·  1987–1991
✦

In 1987 Smiths Food and Drug decided to open a new region of stores in the Tucson area and selected me as one of the management personnel. So we packed up the house in West Valley, put it on the market, and headed south — trading Utah winters for the Sonoran Desert, trading familiar wards and neighborhoods for entirely new ones, trusting as we always had that the Lord would make something good of wherever we landed.

Kathy had to pack and load the U-Haul practically by herself, since I had to get down there right away. Her parents Jim and Verna drove down with her — all the way from West Valley to Tucson, pulling a loaded trailer. When they arrived at the store I gave them a quick tour of the new facility and then we all drove out to the house on the Old Spanish Trail, southeast of the city.

McKay rode with me. He had grown so much while I was getting things set up in Tucson that I barely recognized him — he was just two years old, and Adam had barely turned four. When Kathy's parents finally said their goodbyes and headed back to Utah, Kathy wept on my shoulder. I picked her up and twirled her around a few times while Jim and Verna could still see her, and they waved from the car and pretended to be happy for us. It helped for a few moments.

The welcome to Tucson
It was November. The boys stripped off their coats and jumped straight into the backyard pool.
The water was still warm from the sun. It was eighty-three degrees.
A neighbor came over to politely inform us it was winter and generally considered too cold to swim.
"Does it ever get really cold here?" Dee asked. "Oh yes," the neighbor said. "Sometimes it drops clear down into the sixties."

We prayed together before Jim and Verna left and we both felt that this would be a good mission for our family — that the Lord had something for us in Arizona. Kathy found that belief hard to hold in the first weeks, alone most of the day while I worked long hours getting the new store off the ground. But she was faithful about it, as she was faithful about most things, and the ward wrapped around us quickly. She started inviting families over to use the pool and soon the house was full again, which is how she liked it.

· · ·

One of the first people we met was named Dusty. Another was named Sandy. We took this as a sign we were in the right place.

Kathy developed something during the Tucson years that had been present in her all along but found its full expression in the desert: a gift for elderly women who were widowed and lonely. She found them at stores, struck up conversations, and before she knew it had their addresses. She would take Adam and McKay with her and go visit — sitting with these women, caring for them, becoming their friend. Several of them crossed the veil with Kathy present. She was that kind of person.

The yard was completely fenced, which meant coyotes and javelinas — the wild desert pigs that roam the Sonoran — couldn't get in. They would come to the gate and attempt to tempt our dog Rambo into coming out to fight. We also had a family of kangaroo rats living in the yard, and a family of Gambel's Quail, until Rambo dispatched the quail. We tried not to hold this against him.

· · ·

I have to tell you about the pool.

We had neighbors over regularly that summer, and in one instance a mother allowed her three-year-old son to go out to the pool without supervision. The next thing we knew, his older brother came screaming into the house that the little boy was floating at the bottom.

What happened next
"We ran out and pulled the boy up. I was able to resuscitate him. It was a very close call. I learned my lesson about adult supervision when children are near water — a lesson I have never forgotten and never needed to learn again."

The Lord was merciful that afternoon. The boy went home alive. I went inside and sat down and stayed there for a while.

· · ·

In 1990 I was finally promoted to store director in Sierra Vista, a town closer to the Mexican border. It was what I had been working toward for years — my own store, my own operation, and the promise from the regional office of a genuine future with the company. Kathy and I were excited. We would finally be able to attend church together on Sundays, something the management schedule had made nearly impossible for years.

Things started well. I began earning bonuses right away. We paid off the cars and cleared our debts. I made plans to start saving for the older boys' missions.

Then the trouble started. My assistant was demoted. The warehouse kept selling us damaged goods and marking us out of stock on items I could see they had in inventory. When I started investigating I discovered that approvals for our orders were being blocked at the district office level. Something was wrong, and the more I poked at it the more it smelled wrong.

My attempts to solve the problem eventually put me on the wrong side of a vice president's girlfriend. Within a short time I was demoted, transferred back to Tucson, and assigned to the night crew. I lost approximately fifty thousand dollars a year in salary in a single day.

What Kathy was doing through all of this

While Dee was navigating the professional collapse in Sierra Vista, Kathy was called as Relief Society president — the women's organization leader for the ward. Her sisters were spread forty miles across the desert. She served them faithfully anyway. She always did.

In spite of everything that went wrong at work, even that one year in Sierra Vista gave us some of the richest experiences of service we had ever had. We taught investigators as stake missionaries. I taught elders quorum and led the ward choir. Kathy made friends quickly and was genuinely happy in the ward, which was its own kind of grace given everything else that was happening.

"The Lord seemed to be making a consistent point throughout those Arizona years: that the quality of a life is not determined by whether the professional circumstances cooperate. It is determined by whether you show up and serve wherever you are planted."

We showed up. We served. And eventually a friendly contact at Smith's headquarters in Salt Lake found a position for me at the St. George store, and we loaded the largest U-Haul we could find and drove north out of the Sonoran Desert, pulling a car behind us.

As we came out of the gorge approaching St. George, I saw something I hadn't seen in years.

Green.

Most Utahns would not consider St. George particularly green. Compared to the Sonoran Desert, it was the most beautiful sight I had ever seen. My eyes were so happy I didn't have words for it. Kathy laughed at me from the passenger seat.

We were going home.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa and Grandma Kathy spent about three years in Tucson and about one year in Sierra Vista, Arizona. The boys jumped into the pool in November because the water was still warm. Kathy visited lonely elderly women and sat with them when they died. Grandpa resuscitated a neighbor's toddler from the bottom of the pool. He worked his way up to store director and then lost most of his salary in a day due to company politics. Through all of it, they served in the ward and made friends and kept going. When they finally drove north to St. George and saw green again, Grandpa nearly wept with happiness.
