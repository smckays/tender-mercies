The Night the Rain Stopped

Hill Cumorah, Palmyra, New York · Summer, 1969

In upstate New York, it rains about every other day. Not a gentle mist — real rain, the kind that comes sideways and makes the sky go green. Everybody in Palmyra knew this, which made the tradition all the more remarkable: in thirty-seven years, the Hill Cumorah Pageant had never once been rained out.

Thirty thousand people would come on a given night. They drove from Ohio, from Pennsylvania, from New England — some of them not members of the Church, just people who had heard about the production and wanted to see it. They'd spread blankets on the hillside and watch the story of the Book of Mormon play out across a stage the size of a city block, with lights and water curtains and a cast of hundreds. And at the end, the figure of Jesus Christ descending from the night sky.

So when the storm came that evening — a real storm, the sky going green, the thunder rolling across the hills in long slow waves — what we felt was not just weather. It felt personal.

The visiting authority that night was Elder Stapely of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. When the rain intensified and the pageant president made the official announcement that the performance would not proceed, the crowd began moving toward the parking lots. Thirty thousand people, folding up their blankets in the rain.

Elder Stapely called us all together — cast, crew, missionaries — back in the trees behind the stage. I was eighteen years old and I had never been this close to an apostle of the Lord. I remember the quiet that came over us as he stood among us in the rain.

He didn't give a speech. He used his priesthood, and he commanded the rain to cease.

"It was as if the giant taps of heaven were shut off suddenly, and not another drop fell."

— Dee Stevens

I have tried many times over the years to find words adequate for what happened next. The closest I can get is this: it was as if someone had reached up and turned off a faucet. One moment the rain was falling. The next moment it was not. No tapering off, no gradual slowing. The rain simply stopped.

We stood there in the sudden silence for a moment, all of us, looking at each other and at the sky. Then we ran.

Everyone ran to their places. The audience, who had been halfway to their cars, turned around and came back. The pageant went forward that night without another drop falling.

At the end, as the audience filtered slowly out into the night, a small cloud drifted over the hill. A single gentle peal of thunder rolled across the sky — soft, almost courteous — as if to let us know whose production this actually was.

The next morning the Palmyra newspaper ran a headline in bold type: Mormon Luck Holds Out Again.

We laughed at that. Luck. After what we had witnessed in those trees, the word felt like calling the ocean a puddle. But I have thought about that headline many times since. Maybe luck is just what it looks like from the outside when the Lord moves quietly enough that only the people standing in the trees know what really happened.

I was one of those people. I was eighteen years old, standing in the rain, watching an apostle of the Lord command the heavens — and being answered. That is not a thing you forget.

Whenever life went dark and the rain seemed like it might never stop, I knew something I had learned standing in those trees: the Lord is not watching from a distance. He is right there in the storm with you. And when He speaks, even heaven listens.
A Note for the Grandkids

Grandpa was 18 when this happened — younger than some of you reading this right now. He was standing in those trees when Elder Stapely commanded the rain to stop. He watched it stop. The Palmyra newspaper called it luck. Grandpa knew better.
