The Initiation

D
Dee Stevens Elk Mountain  ·  Blanding, Utah  ·  1962
✦

The custom among most scout troops, at least in that part of Utah in 1962, was to initiate new members. The method was straightforward: the entire troop would swarm a new boy and tear off his pants. Dee would be the first to acknowledge this sounds somewhat barbaric. It was not a highly civilized region of the state.

One evening we were out on Elk Mountain with a large bonfire going and food cooking over it — the kind of outdoor meal that tastes better than anything a kitchen has ever produced, because you are twelve years old and the air smells like juniper and the fire makes the darkness feel friendly instead of threatening.

Then it was decided the time had come to initiate the new boys.

This is where my legs entered the story.

· · ·

One thing I had developed over my years in the desert was a pair of running legs that nobody in that troop could match. When I took off through the sagebrush, not a single one of them stood a flea's chance of catching me, and they all knew it. They scattered after the other new boys first — which made sense, since they couldn't get everyone at once and there was no point chasing me until they had more numbers available.

I crouched behind a large sagebrush and watched them all pour past me in pursuit of the other recruits. The clothing I was wearing that evening happened to be grey and dark blue, which blended into the dusk as if I had planned it, which I had not. I circled wide around the camp, staying low, and found a dark spot behind a log near the fire where I could lie flat and watch everything happening without anyone seeing me.

I stayed there for an hour.

What he heard from behind the log
The troop gathered around the fire. The other new boys had been initiated. The search for Dee had been given up.
Leonard Hurst said: "Seems as though Dee has outfoxed you all. I don't think you should have to initiate him — he's proven himself in my book."
They all began calling out for him to come in. They had decided not to pants him. He was safe to show himself.
He was close enough to the fire to hear every word.

I stood up from behind the log and walked toward them.

The looks on their faces when they understood how close I had been the whole time — that was worth the hour on the cold ground. I felt like a genuine champion. They patted me on the back. They congratulated me. Leonard said the closing prayer and we all stood around the fire in what I can only describe as brotherhood, the way boys feel brotherhood when something has gone right in the wilderness and everyone is warm and fed and the night is full of stars.

Immediately after the prayer
In that same spirit of brotherhood, they all pounced on him at once and he got the initiation same as anyone else.

In retrospect, I should have seen that coming.

· · ·

I have told this story many times over sixty years and it never fails to get a laugh, which is exactly what it deserves. But there is something underneath the comedy that I think about more as I get older.

Leonard Hurst said I had proven myself. He said that in front of the whole troop, without knowing I was listening. He was not performing for me. He believed it.

I was eleven years old and I had not done anything particularly heroic. I had just run fast and hid well. But Leonard chose to see in that something worth recognizing — initiative, self-reliance, the refusal to submit to what everyone else assumed was inevitable. He named it out loud in front of my peers, and something in me took note of it and grew a little taller.

"The things a good leader says about you when he thinks you can't hear — those are the ones that stay."

Who Leonard Hurst was

He was rugged and demanding, and he loved the outdoors the way a man loves something he has built his life around. Under Leonard, the boys of Blanding stopped thinking of scouting as something you did to earn badges and started thinking of it as a serious education in how to handle yourself in the world. He was the biggest influence of Dee's first year in Blanding. The blue ribbon from the Sand Island camporee was hauled directly to his house so he could see it. He died in 2014. Kathy was assigned to visit him in his final years and he still remembered that boy who had outfoxed the whole troop on Elk Mountain.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa was eleven years old when this happened. The whole scout troop was coming to tear off his pants as part of the initiation tradition, and he outran every one of them, circled around camp, and hid behind a log in the dark for an hour close enough to the fire to hear everything they said. The scoutmaster declared him exempt. They agreed. Then they got him anyway the second the prayer was over. He has been laughing about it ever since. The part he thinks about most is what Leonard Hurst said about him when he thought no one was listening.
