Part Two: Love, Loss, and Starting Over  ·  Chapter Thirteen

Keri Lyn

D
Dee Stevens Provo, Utah  ·  1973–1975
❧

I want to tell you about my daughter Keri Lyn — not her death, which comes later and deserves its own accounting, but her life. The particular texture of it. The way she arrived in the world and what she was like in it, before everything else happened.

She was born September 8th, 1973. She had the sweetest little temperament from the very first days — calm and watchful and somehow already herself in a way that some babies are and some are not. She crawled and walked on schedule. She was learning to talk. She was becoming a person in front of us at the ordinary speed that children become people, and I adored every minute of it.

How I adored that child. I want to say that plainly, before anything else.

· · ·

Then she got sick.

It started with a fever — the kind that climbs and won't come down no matter what you do. We watched her, and worried, and did what young parents do: we hoped it would pass. It didn't pass. She began to lose weight. She lost her ability to walk. She lost her words, one by one, until she couldn't talk anymore. The bright, present little person who had been becoming herself in front of us went somewhere we couldn't follow.

We took her to Primary Children's Hospital.

What the doctors found
Achemotaxis
She had plenty of white blood cells. They simply failed to go fight off infection — like an army that couldn't receive its orders. Her body could not defend itself. Every illness that reached her found no resistance waiting.

She was a very sick baby. We prayed and fasted. We had her administered to. We sat beside her hospital bed and asked the Lord, as plainly as we knew how, to let her stay.

Gradually — slowly, in the way that recoveries happen when you are watching every small sign — she came back. The weight returned. The walking returned. The words came back one by one, like lights being switched back on in a house that had gone dark.

What we knew afterward

"We knew it was a tender mercy from the Lord. And we were yet to learn that Keri's life was a complete set of tender mercies — one after another, stacked up like gifts, more than her short years should have been able to hold."

· · ·

I have thought many times about the particular mercy of those early years with Keri Lyn — the years before the harder things arrived. We were given time with her that we almost didn't get. Every morning she woke up and came padding into the kitchen was a morning we might not have had.

Parents of healthy children do not always know to count these things. They assume the ordinary mornings will keep coming — the cereal on the table, the small voice asking for something, the weight of a child climbing into your lap. They assume this because it is reasonable to assume. It had always been true before.

We had been taught, by one fever and one long hospital stay, not to assume. To receive each ordinary morning as what it was: a gift. Not guaranteed. Not owed to us. Given.

"She was returned to us. That is the only way I know to say it. We asked and she was returned. Whatever came after, I have never stopped being grateful for the years we got."

She grew. She became herself again — and more than herself, because children who come through something hard often do. There was a quality to Keri Lyn that I recognized but couldn't name: a kind of earnestness, a presence, as if she understood, even without words for it, that being here was not something to take lightly.

She had surgeries. She had learning struggles. She faced more than her share of hard things in her ten years, and most of them are woven through the chapters that follow this one. But this chapter is not about those things. This chapter is about the girl herself — the sweetness of her temperament, the way she lit up a room without trying, the particular mercy of having her at all.

I am her father. I adored her from the first moment and I adore her still, across whatever distance now lies between us.

That is what I want you to know about Keri Lyn before anything else.

What comes next

The next chapter covers the drive to Spokane — the mountain pass, the trailer with no brakes, and the moment on a quiet stretch of highway that neither Dee nor her mother has ever been able to fully explain. Keri Lyn is not quite two years old. She is already being watched over by someone other than her parents.

A Note for the Grandkids Keri Lyn was Grandpa's firstborn child. She got very sick as a baby — sick enough that they almost lost her — and then she recovered. Grandpa has always counted those recovered years as a specific mercy from the Lord. She is buried in West Jordan, Utah. She was ten years old when she died, and Grandpa has never stopped missing her. This chapter is just about who she was. The rest of her story comes later.
