Part One · The Making of a Young Man Chapter Three

The Call I Prayed For

Provo, Utah · Winter, 1969–1970

When I came home from that first summer at Hill Cumorah, I was not the same person who had left. Something had settled into me over those months — in the grass at the base of the light tower, on the stages during the pageant, in the Sacred Grove on the last morning — something I did not have a precise name for but could feel the weight of every day. A sense of being known. Of being watched over. Of having been placed, very deliberately, exactly where I was supposed to be.

What do you do with a feeling like that? For me, the answer was simple: you go on a mission.

In the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, young men are encouraged to serve two-year missions at around age nineteen — going out in pairs to wherever they are called, teaching, testifying, knocking on doors. There are, or were in 1969, sixty-nine missions in the Church worldwide. A young man fills out his paperwork, submits it to Church headquarters, and then waits. The call can come from anywhere.

My bishop smiled at me like a patient uncle when I told him I wanted to be called to the Cumorah Mission — the one based in upstate New York, covering the very ground I had just spent a summer on. He didn't say it was impossible. He just smiled in the way that people smile when they want to be kind about something unlikely.

I heard what the smile meant. I submitted my paperwork anyway. And then I got on my knees.

  • Night 1 Please send me to Cumorah.
  • Night 7 Please send me to Cumorah.
  • Night 14 Please send me to Cumorah.
  • Night 30 Please send me to Cumorah.
  • Night 45 I will go wherever You send me. I will accept whatever comes. But — please. Send me to Cumorah.

My father had moved our family to California by then. I was living alone with my Grandma Stevens in Provo so I could finish out the year. The house was quiet in the evenings. I did my schoolwork, helped Grandma where I could, and prayed the same prayer every night with what I hoped was the right combination of faith and surrender.

· · ·

The letter arrived on an ordinary afternoon. There was nothing remarkable about the envelope from the outside. Grandma stood nearby. I picked it up and my hands began to shake before I had even broken the seal.

Official Mission Call · Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints
Elder Dee Stevens
Provo, Utah

"You are hereby called to serve as a missionary for the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. You are assigned to labor in the Cumorah Mission."

David O. McKay · Prophet and President of the Church

"I lost control of my legs."

One moment he was standing in Grandma's living room. The next he was on the floor, yelling — I don't believe it!

Grandma's tears told me what I had read was true. Of the eighteen young men who had served on the Cumorah work crew that summer, I was the only one called to that mission.

Just me.

He didn't send me to Cumorah because I had earned it. He sent me because He knew me — and He knew that I needed to go back to understand what I had only begun to learn.

The practice of bringing your full honest self to prayer — asking for what you actually want while releasing the outcome — is one of the most useful things I have ever learned. I have returned to it hundreds of times since that winter in Grandma's house. When Kathy was sick. When children were lost. When I was on dialysis and didn't know if I would see another year. When I was alone in a house that still smelled like the woman I had loved for forty-two years.

Every time, the practice was the same: ask plainly. Release honestly. Trust the one who is listening. It always ends with an answer. I have staked my whole life on that, and I have never once been wrong.

A Note for the Grandkids

Grandpa prayed for a specific mission call every single night for weeks, living alone with his grandmother in Provo while his family was in California. When the letter arrived, his legs gave out and he ended up on the living room floor. His call was signed by the prophet David O. McKay. Of all eighteen boys who'd worked that summer at Cumorah, he was the only one sent back. He has never thought that was an accident.
