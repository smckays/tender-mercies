The Summer That Changed Everything

Hill Cumorah, Palmyra, New York · Summer, 1969

I was eighteen years old and sprawled in the grass at the base of a seventy-five-foot light tower when the block fell.

It was the summer of 1969 and I was one of eighteen young men from Utah County who had been called to spend the summer at Hill Cumorah in Palmyra, New York — a sacred site for members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, where the prophet Joseph Smith said he received the golden plates that became the Book of Mormon. Every summer the Church staged a massive outdoor pageant there, and that year they were rebuilding the hill from the ground up: new sod up the entire face, new light towers, new wiring, new stages hauled out of storage and assembled and painted.

We were the muscle. Eighteen boys, fresh out of high school, working from dawn until long after dark in the upstate New York heat. I had never worked so hard in my life, and I had never felt so certain that what I was doing mattered.

On the afternoon it happened, a few of the crew were using a block and tackle to haul equipment up to one of the light towers — seventy-five feet straight up, a long way to watch something fall. I had just taken a short break and laid myself down in the new grass at the base of the tower, arms out, face to the sky.

Then the air changed.

"It was as if the air was sucked away — and suddenly there were horrified faces staring down at me from above."

One second · two seconds · three

The block came crashing down and landed near my hand, brushing past me as it slid through the grass.

From where the crew was standing it must have looked like a certain death. A boy laid out flat, directly beneath a falling piece of metal equipment. I heard later they were sure I was gone.

But here is what I can tell you from where I was lying: I felt, in the moment before the block hit, something settle around me. The only word I have ever found for it is a bubble — as if a protective bubble was placed around me, and the block simply could not reach what was inside it.

I have carried a particular testimony ever since that afternoon: that as long as I was on the Lord's errand, nothing would happen to me that was not intended. Not that nothing hard would happen. But that I would not be taken before my time, and that the work I had been sent to do would get done.

· · ·

I should tell you about dying.

One of my assignments during pageant week was to run around the hill during the destruction scene. When a flashing light — simulated lightning — struck me, I was to die like a desperado and fling myself dramatically off the stage. Then get up, run to the next stage, and repeat.

One night I jumped into the darkness from one of the higher stages and just kept going — rolling down the hill, picking up speed, unable to stop, until I arrived with a considerable thud at the lower level where other cast members were waiting in the bushes for the Hill Christ scene.

I laid there for a few seconds, checking to see if I was still assembled correctly, when I heard a sister whisper to someone beside her:

"Do you think he's dead?"

I was not dead. But I had learned something useful about momentum.

July 24th, 1969 · The top of Hill Cumorah

On the evening of the 24th of July I stood at the top of the hill and stared up at the moon. Four days earlier, on July 20th, Neil Armstrong had walked on its surface. I was eighteen years old, standing on a hill in upstate New York, and men were walking on the moon. Two things that seemed impossible were happening to me simultaneously that summer: men were walking on the moon, and I was learning that the Lord was real. Both required believing in something I could not fully see. Both turned out to be true.

On the last day of the summer, as we stood in the Sacred Grove in a circle bearing witness to each other before heading home, something came to me quietly and clearly: I would be back. I didn't know how or when. I just knew.

I was right. I came back twice more. The hill kept calling me back, and I kept going. Some places are like that. Some summers are like that. They become a part of you before you fully understand what has happened.

I am seventy-four years old as I write this. Through everything that has come since — mission, marriage, loss, illness, the death of a woman I loved for forty-two years — there has been one constant. The Lord has been paying attention. Not in a distant, abstract way. In a close, particular, personal way I could feel and sometimes see and have never been able to explain away.

I call these moments tender mercies. They are what this book is about. And this summer is where they started.

A Note for the Grandkids

Grandpa was 18 years old in the summer of 1969 — the same summer men first walked on the moon. He spent it laying sod on a hillside in New York, dying dramatically on a stage every night, and learning for the first time that the Lord was paying close attention to his life. He has never stopped believing that. Everything else in this book comes from that belief.
