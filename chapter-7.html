Part One · The Making of a Young Man Chapter Seven

The Hippies, the Priest, and the Truck Driver

Rochester & Saratoga Springs, New York · 1970–1971

One of the things that surprised me most about missionary work was this: the Lord did not seem particularly concerned about using conventional means to accomplish His purposes. He would use whatever was available. Whatever was at hand. Whatever happened to be driving down the highway at the right moment.

I want to tell you about three people — none of them members of the Church, none of them doing anything you would expect to advance the gospel — who each, in their own unlikely way, opened a door that the missionaries alone could not have opened. They didn't know they were doing it. That was, I think, rather the point.

· · ·
Story One The Hippie Van Palmyra, New York · Summer, 1970 · 6:47 in the morning

Elder Marion D. Hanks was attending the pageant that summer and had asked the entire cast to meet with him in the Sacred Grove the following morning. I had worked most of the night and managed only about four hours of sleep before I had to be up and suited. I threw myself together and raced out to the road to catch the bus — and missed it by a matter of minutes.

The Grove was miles away. The meeting was starting. I had no car and no options, so I started walking briskly down the highway toward Palmyra and hoped for the best.

Within moments a vehicle rolled up beside me. It was a micro-mini-bus painted in every psychedelic color known to man — purples and oranges and swirling greens, the kind of paint job that announces its owner's philosophy before anyone says a word. The windows rolled down. Inside were four authentic hippies, and the smell that came out of that van was unmistakably marijuana.

I stood there on the shoulder of the highway in my missionary suit, doing the mathematics. The Grove was far. The meeting had started. The van was here.

I climbed in.

They were perfectly polite. They asked no questions and made no remarks about the suit. They drove me right to the Joseph Smith home and dropped me off with a cheerful wave. I walked into the Sacred Grove smelling considerably more interesting than I had when I left the barn, and I made it just in time.

The Lord needed me in that grove. He had four hippies and a psychedelic van available. He used what He had.

Elder Hanks was already speaking when I slipped in. He welcomed us by listing the names of every prophet of this dispensation who had previously passed on — as if they too were present and accounted for. The Spirit in that grove was extraordinary. He assured us that the pageant being rained out the previous night had nothing to do with our collective righteousness, and reminded us that the Lord causes rain to fall on the righteous and the wicked alike.

I have thought about those four hippies many times since. They have no idea they delivered a missionary to one of the most spiritually significant mornings of his mission. They were just going wherever they were going. The Lord redirected them by approximately thirty seconds to pick up a boy in a suit on a highway — and then sent them on their way.

Story Two Father Tierney Saratoga Springs, New York · 1971 · The local Catholic parish

When I arrived in Saratoga Springs as a senior companion, my new companion Elder Maxwell introduced me to Father Tierney — the Catholic priest of the local parish. He was older, soft-spoken, and had, somewhat improbably, a personal acquaintance with Elder Mark E. Peterson of the Quorum of the Twelve. Once while the elders were visiting his office, he actually dialed up Elder Peterson and let them speak with him briefly. This was not typical behavior for a Catholic priest toward a pair of Mormon missionaries.

Father Tierney had a genuine interest in the elders and their work. When they told him that many of the people they were meeting in their tracting were Catholics from his own parish, he smiled and said perhaps he should go tracting with them so he could meet some of these Catholics himself.

Then he did something no one anticipated.

He invited the elders to attend one of his Sunday services. They went. And before Father Tierney began his sermon that morning, he asked the missionaries to stand. He turned to his congregation and said:

What Father Tierney said to his congregation

"I would like to introduce the Mormon elders in our area. They will be knocking on your doors in the near future with a message. I would advise you to listen to what they have to say." — Father Tierney, Catholic priest, Saratoga Springs, New York

Elder Maxwell reported that their success in door-to-door contacting improved quite considerably after that Sunday. People opened their doors. People invited them in. People said, with some curiosity, that their priest had mentioned them.

Father Tierney was transferred to an office position not long afterward — a new young priest came in, held rock masses, attendance went up — and the particular window he had opened quietly closed. But for a season, a Catholic priest had done more for the missionary work in Saratoga Springs than the missionaries had managed to do on their own.

I have always believed he was acting on something he felt, even if he couldn't have named what it was. Some people have that — a sense of where truth is moving, and the courage to point toward it even when it isn't their own tradition doing the moving.

Story Three The Man in the Laundromat Somewhere in Ohio · 2:00 in the morning · A long-haul truck stop

This one I did not witness directly. It came to us secondhand, through the bishop of the Rochester ward, who received a phone call in the middle of the night.

A truck driver was on a long haul and had stopped somewhere in Ohio to do his laundry. While he waited, he found a copy of the Book of Mormon sitting on one of the tables — left there by someone, for someone. He picked it up. He began to read.

The Spirit moved on him so strongly that by the time his laundry was done he could not put the book down. He kept reading as he drove. By the time he pulled into Rochester it was the middle of the night, but he could not wait until morning. He found a phone booth, looked up the church that had printed the book, and found the bishop's number listed.

He called. The bishop answered.

What the truck driver said

He had questions. He needed the answers now. Not tomorrow. Not next week. Now. The bishop spent the rest of the night answering his questions and bearing his witness. A new man drove off in his truck that morning, anxious to get back home and be baptized.

Someone had left a Book of Mormon in an Ohio laundromat. They had no idea who would find it. They would never know what happened next. The truck driver had no idea, when he sat down to wait for his clothes, that his life was about to change. The bishop had no idea, when he went to bed that night, that his phone would ring before sunrise with a man who needed answers right now.

None of them knew. The Lord knew.

· · ·

I tell you these three stories together because I think they illustrate something important about how the work actually moves — not in straight lines, not always through the expected channels, not only through missionaries in suits knocking on doors in the middle of the afternoon.

It moves through hippies who happen to be driving the right direction. Through priests who feel something they can't fully explain and act on it anyway. Through a book left on a table in a laundromat by someone who will never know what they set in motion.

The Lord is not limited to the tools we hand Him. He will use whatever is at hand. Our job is simply to be at hand — available, prepared, willing to be used — and trust that He will arrange the rest.

I have tried to live by that. I have tried to be the kind of person who is available to be redirected — who doesn't grip the plan so tightly that he misses the hippie van pulling over on the highway. Who stays loose enough to be useful.

It is not always easy. But it is almost always interesting.

A Note for the Grandkids

Three stories from Grandpa's mission that have nothing to do with missionaries doing missionary things — and everything to do with the Lord using whatever He has available. A van full of hippies. A Catholic priest with the courage to point his congregation toward truth he recognized but didn't own. A truck driver reading a book at 2am in an Ohio laundromat. The Lord arranged all of it. Grandpa just had to show up.
