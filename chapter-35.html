Part Five: Letting Go  ·  Chapter Thirty-Five

Elora Lyn

D
Dee Stevens Pleasant Grove, Utah  ·  2007
✦

McKay came home from his mission to Mexico in the spring of 2007, and the whole family came to the airport. That is the kind of thing this family does — we show up, all of us, for the arrivals and the departures, the homecomings and the farewells. Travis and Krista were there with their children: Caleb, Shaustia, Walker, and little Elora Lyn, who was two years old.

We have pictures from that day. In one of them, Ellie is holding a welcome home sign, beaming the way two-year-olds beam when they are part of something important and know it. It is a happy photograph. It is one of the last ones of her well.

She had been showing signs of something for a little while — a painful illness no one had yet named. That night, after the homecoming, after the hugs and the stories and the particular joy of having a missionary back in the room, little Ellie ended up at Primary Children's Hospital.

They found a brain tumor.

It was inoperable.

She passed away that night.

· · ·

I do not have words adequate to what Travis and Krista went through that night, and in the weeks and months and years that followed. I am her grandfather and I felt the loss deeply. They are her parents. There is no comparison to be made between those two experiences. What I know of what they carried, I know from watching them carry it — from the outside, from a distance, from the place where grandparents stand when their children are suffering something they cannot fix.

Krista, over the eight years that followed, began writing. She recorded her thoughts about Elora — about that night, about grief and faith and the long work of learning to let go of a child. The writing was honest and difficult and took the better part of a decade to complete. It became a book.

Unbearable Burden

Krista Walker Stevens spent eight years writing about the night they lost Elora Lyn and the years that followed. The book is called Unbearable Burden. It is, as Dee describes it, a touching story about what it means to let go of a child — to hold faith in one hand and grief in the other and keep walking forward. It exists because Krista had the courage to sit with the hardest thing and put it into words, and because she believed that words might help someone else do the same.

It is available and worth reading. It is not an easy book. It is an honest one.

· · ·

Elora Lyn is buried in the American Fork Cemetery. Two of her uncles — Benjamin and Jared — have since been buried there as well. Three members of this family, in the same ground, all of them gone before their time by any accounting the living would make.

I have thought often about what it means that they are together there. I do not think it means the Lord made a mistake. I think it means the Lord's accounting is different from ours — that what looks like early from this side may look entirely different from the other side, where Elora has been waiting all this time, and where Jared and Ben arrived to find a niece they had loved.

American Fork Cemetery
"Our little angel granddaughter passed away that night. She is buried in American Fork. Benjamin and Jared, two of her uncles, have since joined her there."

The day McKay came home was one of the happiest days of that year. Elora held her sign and the whole family was together and a missionary walked through the doors and everyone was present for it. That happened. The photograph is real. The joy of that afternoon was real.

And then the evening came and brought what it brought.

I have lived long enough to know that this is sometimes how it goes — that the Lord does not always separate the happy days from the hard ones, that sometimes the joy and the grief arrive in the same twenty-four hours, in the same family, in the same airport. I do not know why He arranges it that way sometimes. I know that He does.

"We have the pictures from that day. Ellie holding her sign. Everyone together. That afternoon was real, and it was good, and nothing that came after it erases what it was."

Elora Lyn was two years old. She had Caleb and Shaustia and Walker for siblings, and Travis and Krista for parents, and a grandfather who thinks about her still. She held a welcome home sign at an airport on the day her uncle came home from his mission. She was beaming.

That is the picture I keep.
