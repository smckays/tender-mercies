A Life of Tender Mercies  ·  Dee Stevens
Part Three: Kathy  ·  Chapter Twenty-Three

The Shaft, and What Came After

D
Dee Stevens St. George, Utah  ·  1991–1996
✦

When we arrived in St. George we were in bad shape financially. The demotion in Arizona had cost us deeply, and we arrived in southern Utah cramped into a two-bedroom condo in Dixie Downs, spreading mattresses on the floor at night so everyone had somewhere to sleep and standing them against the walls in the morning so we could walk. The city felt like a fresh start. The living situation felt like a test.

Then I got sick. Some kind of attack in my gut, so painful that Kathy had me hospitalized for observation. They could not determine what was wrong and released me still in pain. That night Kathy put her arm around me in bed and the pain disappeared. Just like that — her arm around me, and it was gone. I slept peacefully. In the morning I was well enough to go to work. I have always counted that among the tenderest of the tender mercies.

Part of the Lord's mercy for us men, I have always believed, is the gift of a faithful wife. Some gifts are grand and unmistakable. Some arrive through a woman's arm in the dark.

· · ·

We needed a house. The condo was not sustainable with a family our size. One Sunday we decided as a family to fast together — to put our petition to the Lord formally and specifically, the way we had learned to pray over the years. We asked for heavenly help finding an affordable home. That afternoon there was a knock at the door.

Standing on the porch were my Uncle Dale and Aunt Janeen. They had driven from Lancaster, California. To this day I have no idea how they found us — we were not on anyone's phone list, and this was before the age of easy tracking. They had simply felt prompted to come.

Aunt Janeen said: "We have come to help you find a house."

They stayed until we had found a wonderful home and signed the papers. They arranged the down payment. Five bedrooms, three bathrooms, a living room, a family room with a fireplace, a kitchen, a laundry room, and a garage. Everyone in the family had their own space. We lived two doors down from our bishop. It was, as directly as I can say it, an answer to prayer that arrived before the day was out.

The sequence of events on that Sunday
Morning: the family fasted and prayed for help finding a home. Afternoon: a knock at the door. Evening: Uncle Dale and Aunt Janeen, who had driven from California, were sitting in the living room. They had come to help find a house. They did not leave until it was found.
· · ·

St. George was a happy place for us. We lived there five years and a great deal happened — to us and through us and for our boys, who were growing up fast now and becoming people in their own right.

What those five years contained
Travis worked at Smiths with Dee and ran the ice cream program with distinction. The company sent him to Boys State for his leadership. He was called to Russia.
Jared worked at a Mexican restaurant whose owner loved him enough to help pay for his mission to Chile.
Michael was called to Brazil. He and Jared served simultaneously in South America.
Kathy was hired by Barbara Greene to manage the Greene Gate Village Bed and Breakfast — cleaning staff, kitchen, special events. She hosted Governor Leavitt and his entourage one night.
Dee was released from the stake mission and called as Young Men's President while Travis, Jared, and Michael were in Young Men's — and then watched all three receive mission calls.
Ben fell fifteen feet off the roof onto the driveway. His eye came out of its socket. A neighbor administered to him. A helicopter took him to Primary Hospital. They re-inserted the eye successfully. He passed the sacrament at a hospital meeting before coming home.
Dee coached little league for Adam and McKay. One year the team took the city championship.
· · ·

Ben's story deserves more than a line in a list. It deserves its own chapter and it will get one. But I want to say something here about what it meant to administer to him in the hospital with another trusted neighbor, promising him by the Spirit that he would be healed — and then watching it happen. His eye was re-inserted successfully. He went home. He grew up to become deeply invested in Boy Scouts, serving as an assistant scoutmaster well into his twenties.

The Lord was keeping track of Ben. He kept close track of all of them, in ways I often didn't see until years later.

"I coached those boys, prayed over those boys, worried about those boys, and watched them grow into men. That is the work of a father. It does not end. It just changes shape."

A story about a home teacher and a mission

In Sierra Vista, Dee had been assigned to home teach a young man of mission age who had stopped attending church. The young man did his best to avoid Dee. Dee tracked him to a shoe store and managed brief regular visits before being transferred. Years later, in St. George, the young man's father approached Dee to report that his son had decided to serve a mission. He asked his parents what had changed. He told them it was his home teacher. Dee had thought his efforts were meager. The Holy Ghost had been doing the real work.

After five years in St. George, I was offered a position as assistant manager in Kingman, Arizona. The store was successful and the bonuses were considerable. It was a hard decision — we had built something real in St. George, with friends and callings and roots — but after much prayer we accepted the transfer, put the house on the market, and began packing.

At the last minute, the day before we were set to leave, the company called to say they were sending us not to Kingman but to Bullhead City — a store that had not turned a profit in over ten years. We gritted our teeth and headed for Bullhead, trusting as we always had that something good would come of wherever we were sent.

It did. But that is the next chapter.

A Note for the Grandkids St. George was one of the happiest seasons of Grandpa and Grandma Kathy's life. They arrived broke and cramped into a tiny condo, fasted for help finding a home, and by evening had family from California sitting in the living room ready to help them find one. They spent five years there watching your dads and uncles grow up, get mission calls, and become men. Ben fell off a roof and lost an eye and got it put back. Grandpa coached little league and took the city championship. Grandma ran a bed and breakfast and once hosted the governor. Then they headed for Bullhead City, which was not what anyone had planned.
