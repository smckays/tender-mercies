A Life of Tender Mercies  ·  Dee Stevens
Part Five: Letting Go  ·  Chapter Thirty-Two

Kathy

D
Dee Stevens Orem, Utah  ·  2022–2023
✦

On Sunday, May 15th, 2022, we had the privilege of giving a name and a blessing to little Nicholas Lane Brown — son of Nicholas Cole and Shaustia Isaacson Brown, our first great-grandchild. He will go by Lane. I sat in that meeting and thought: the Lord has allowed me to live long enough for this. It was a full and happy day.

A few weeks later, a terrible thing happened.

· · ·

Kathy was found to have an aggressive brain tumor.

The discovery process required a biopsy. As a result of the procedure she lost the use of her left eye and could no longer drive. She had spent forty years driving herself wherever she needed to go — to the store, to the temple, to pick up someone else's child, to visit someone who needed visiting. The car keys sitting on the counter became their own kind of grief, separate from everything else.

We were given a prognosis of eight to ten months.

Orem, Utah  ·  Spring 2022
Eight to ten months. That was what the doctors said.
Kathy heard it. Then she kept herself faithful by imagining herself held in the arms of Jesus — and asked a granddaughter to paint her there, so she could keep it in front of her every day.
None of us dared hope she would make it until Christmas.
She sailed through the entire winter and into the summer.

Her doctor prescribed a treatment intended to make her more comfortable. Almost immediately the tumor shrank. For several months she had what felt like a new lease on life. We knew it was another of the Father's tender mercies — not a cure, but time. More time than the prognosis had offered. Time for the family to gather. Time for her to be herself a little longer.

I was her caretaker around the clock from the beginning. I cut pads for her feet when they became painful. I drove her wherever she needed to go. By early summer of 2023 the only place she went was to doctors. She put up the bravest, strongest fight I have ever seen a person wage — quiet and faithful and without self-pity, holding the painting Shaustia had made for her, imagining herself already held.

"The Spirit of the Lord was so strong in our home. The family often gathered around her. I was her 24/7 caretaker. It was the privilege of my life."

By early summer it was evident her strength was dwindling. We did not say it plainly to each other. We did not need to. You live with someone for forty-two years and you learn to read what they cannot say.

· · ·

In mid-July of 2023 a call came that shook our whole family.

Our son Jared had experienced a heart attack. Allyson had found his body in the grass next to the fire pit in their backyard. Several attempts were made to revive him. He was gone. He was forty-seven years old.

Mid-July 2023
Jared had called or visited his mother every single night since her cancer had been found. Every night, without fail, to check on her.
When he died, she seemed inconsolable. She wept for him for days and nights.
One evening Dee heard her on the stairs, calling for him.

Somehow she summoned the strength to stand at his funeral and bear her testimony to the family she loved. It was the last testimony she would ever bear. She stood and she spoke and she meant every word, and everyone in that room knew it was costing her something she did not have left to spend.

The shock of losing Jared seemed to be all she could handle. She had been fighting so hard, for so long — fighting past every deadline the doctors had set, fighting into a summer none of us had expected to have with her. But grief has a weight that illness alone does not, and losing her son broke through something that the tumor had not managed to break. She gradually lost her will to go on fighting.

She passed away quietly in her sleep on July 31st, 2023 — two weeks after Jared.

She is buried in the Timpanogos Memorial Gardens on 400 East in Orem.

· · ·

Losing Kathy was the ultimate loss. I do not have a better word for it than that. The scriptures say it is not good for a man to be alone, and I had always read that as a practical observation. Now I understood it as something else — a description of what happens to the interior of a person when the one who made the home a home is gone.

Every room rings with her spirit. The kitchen where she cooked. The chair where she read. The counter where she kept her things. I became ashamed, in those first weeks, at how much I had taken for granted — how many mornings I had walked past what she had done without seeing it, how many ordinary Tuesday evenings I had not understood were the good ones.

She cooked and cleaned and washed and prayed us to happiness. No matter how large the struggle. No matter how thin the money or how long the odds or how many of us were hurting at once. She held things together with a faith so steady it looked effortless from the outside, and I know now that nothing about it was effortless. She simply never let us see the cost.

What she left in every room

The painting Shaustia made for her — Kathy held in the arms of the Savior — kept in front of her day after day through every month of her illness. She had asked for it because she needed to see where she was going. She was not afraid. She was ready. She wanted to be held, and she believed she would be.

She was right.

· · ·

I soldiered on through the end of that year. The holidays were hard. By the time winter settled in I found that evenings alone had become dark in a way I had not anticipated — a specific, physical dark, the kind that is not helped by turning on more lights.

But I was not, I came to understand, actually alone.

Orem, Utah  ·  Winter 2023
"Every room in my home rings with Kathy's spirit. There was a big hole in my heart. But I know I am not really alone. Tender, tender mercies."

I had a borrowed kidney that had been working for twenty-three years past its predicted lifespan. I had five sons who called and checked in. I had grandchildren and great-grandchildren, including Lane, who had arrived into the world on a happy Sunday in May just weeks before the diagnosis — as if the Lord had arranged one last uncomplicated joy before the hard season began. I had the family history work. I had the ward full of young people who had loved Kathy and who turned their attention toward me in her absence with a generosity I had not expected and could not have arranged.

And I had forty-two years of a woman who had prayed me to happiness through everything. Those years do not disappear when the person does. They are present in every room. They are present in the way I know how to get through a hard evening, which is the way she taught me — quietly, faithfully, one day at a time, trusting that the Lord is keeping track of the larger arc even when you cannot see it from where you are standing.

She is not gone. She is ahead.

I intend to catch up.
