Part Two: Love, Loss, and Starting Over  ·  Chapter Fifteen

A Decade Falls Apart

D
Dee Stevens Utah  ·  1975–1980
❧

I am going to tell you about the hardest years of my life honestly, because I think you deserve that. A memoir that only tells the good parts is not a memoir — it is a performance. And the people I most want to reach with this book are the ones who are sitting in the middle of something hard right now, wondering if it will ever get better. I want them to know: it does. But first, here is what the middle looked like for me.

We came back from Spokane in 1977, after two years in the Pacific Northwest. I had found a job with Macey's grocery stores in Salt Lake and we bought a partially finished home in Kearns and settled in. The twins arrived in December of that year — two months early, and they had a rough go of it at first. Four months in intensive care before we could bring them home. Even then they would stop breathing from time to time and back we would go to Primary Hospital.

We eventually sold the Kearns home and bought a nicer place in Granger — three bedrooms, a beautiful yard, a large garden and fruit trees. From the outside we had a great deal going for us. From the inside, the marriage was in serious trouble.

What the late 1970s looked like, all at once
Keri had major surgery on her right leg to straighten her feet.
Michael wasn't talking — we were taking him to speech therapy at the University of Utah.
A car accident left Linda with a broken leg and Keri — age five — thrown face-first through the side panel window of the station wagon.
Two missionary elders happened to witness the accident. Running to help, they found Keri hanging by her stomach on the jagged glass, not breathing. They administered to her. She came back.
Two suspicious fires in our home.
By the end of the decade, I had been thrown out of my own house on false charges.

I want to be careful here, because some of what happened in those years involves people who are still living, and because I have tried throughout my life to speak plainly without speaking cruelly. What I will say is this: my first wife was deeply unhappy in ways that neither of us fully understood at the time, and unhappiness that goes unaddressed long enough eventually finds a way to make itself known. It found a way. By the end of the 1970s our marriage was over.

The divorce was not clean. Divorces rarely are, but this one was particularly ragged at the edges. There were false accusations. There were custody battles. There were moments I would rather not revisit in detail, not because I am ashamed of how I behaved in them, but because they involved my children, and my children are not the story I am trying to tell here.

What I am trying to tell you is what it felt like to be on the other side of all of it — alone, in my thirties, living in a friend's house in the ward while my children were somewhere else every night.

· · ·

I found solace, as I often have, in service.

There was always some couple in the ward having a hard time — illness, a new baby, some difficulty that made an ordinary week feel impossible. I bought a large stewpot and taught myself to make soup. Then I would make a pot, let it cool, and after dark I would leave it on someone's doorstep. No note. No knock. Just the soup, and then I would disappear back into the night.

What the soup was really about
"I knew I needed some pretty serious blessings and hoped that by caring for others I would somehow rate the Lord's attention. I testify that He is aware of us all — even the humblest of His children. Even a man alone in someone else's house, making soup at midnight and leaving it on strangers' porches."

I prayed fervently each night during those months. Not for my circumstances to improve in any particular way — I had learned enough by then to hold my specific requests loosely — but for help. For the Lord to see me. For something to break open.

I asked Him, specifically and repeatedly, to help me find someone I could love and who could love my children — someone who could give them a home and give me back a reason to come home to it.

"Losing my home was a depressing experience. But I knew the Lord was aware of me. And I knew, even then, that this chapter was not the last one."

· · ·

I want to say one more thing about this period before we move forward into what came next.

It is possible to go through a decade of difficulty and come out the other side without bitterness. I know this because I did it, and I am not a particularly extraordinary person. What I had was a testimony that the Lord was keeping track even when I couldn't see the larger arc. What I had was a stewpot and a ward full of people who needed soup. What I had was the habit, learned over years, of bringing my honest self to prayer and releasing the outcome.

Those things were enough. They are often enough, when nothing else is.

What was coming next

A barber. A threat about haircuts. A woman named Kathy Metcalf who was the mother of three wild little boys. And the beginning of the best chapter of Dee Stevens' life — which starts on the very next page.

A Note for the Grandkids This was the hardest decade of Grandpa's life — a marriage that fell apart, children he couldn't tuck in at night, a home he lost, a divorce that was uglier than it needed to be. He got through it by making soup at midnight and leaving it on people's doorsteps. He prayed every night. He kept going. What came next is worth getting to — but he wanted you to know this part first, so you'd understand what it cost him, and what the Lord eventually gave him in return.
