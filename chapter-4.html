Part One · The Making of a Young Man Chapter Four

Learning to Love Someone You Don't Like

Rochester, New York · 1970

My first mission companion was a fine elder. I want to say that clearly, right at the start, because what follows might make it seem otherwise and that would be unfair to him. Elder Gray was faithful, diligent, knowledgeable, and genuinely trying to make me into a better missionary. He was the district leader. He knew what he was doing. He had every reason to be exacting with a green elder who kept getting things wrong.

He was exacting. Relentlessly, daily, exhaustingly exacting.

We worked in the Jewish section of Rochester, New York — not the easiest ground for two young men in suits to knock on doors. We got virtually no response from the people we tracted. Door after door, day after day, nothing. And each evening during study class, Elder Gray would review my performance and find it wanting. He would tell me these things plainly and expect improvement by morning.

I was nineteen years old, six hundred miles from home, living with a man I could not seem to please, failing at the most important thing I had ever tried to do. I began to gain weight so fast I had to send home for money to buy new suits. I began — God forgive me — to resent him.

I didn't want to resent him. I knew it was wrong. I knew the resentment was eating at exactly the part of me I needed most. But knowing something is wrong and being able to stop it are two very different problems.

· · ·

Then I remembered a lesson from seminary. The teacher had been talking about why mothers love their children so completely, so without reservation. The answer she offered was simple: because of how much they serve them. The love follows the service. You don't serve people because you love them — or not only that. You love them because you serve them. The act of giving creates the feeling of caring.

The idea that changed everything

If service creates love — not just expresses it, but actually generates it — then the way to stop resenting Elder Gray was not to wait until I felt differently about him. It was to start serving him. The feeling would follow the action. It had to.

So I decided to be sneaky about it.

Whenever my companion wasn't watching, I ironed a shirt or two for him. I polished his shoes. To my memory, he never noticed. He never suspected anything. I was careful about that — the sneakiness was the point. This was not about getting credit. It was a private experiment, conducted entirely in secret, to see if a seminary lesson was actually true.

It was true.

Not immediately — not in a single afternoon of shoe polishing — but gradually, over days, something in me began to shift. I started noticing things about Elder Gray I hadn't been able to see before. His genuine commitment to the work. The precision he brought to everything. The way he bore his testimony — plainly, without performance, like a man who had long since stopped needing anyone to believe him.

I found myself, against all expectation, gaining respect for him. I actually found joy in the sneakiness. There was something freeing about a kindness that asked for nothing back.

He never said a word about the shirts. He never mentioned the shoes. I liked it that way. The secret was the whole point.
· · ·

I have used this lesson more times than I can count in the sixty years since that apartment in Rochester. It has never once failed me.

Where this lesson went next
  • The neighbor who frightened Kathy and needed befriending — I took him fishing.
  • The ward member who was difficult in meetings — I found him something to do that used his real skills.
  • The middle school student who stabbed a classmate with a fork — I sat him next to my desk and gave him real work to do.
  • The people who left meals on my porch after Kathy died — I started baking bread for everyone I could think of, because I needed to serve someone.

Every time — every single time — the feeling followed the action. Service is not just something you do because you love someone. It is how you learn to love someone you are not yet able to love on your own.

Start with the shoes. Start with the shirt. Start with whatever small thing is in your hands right now, done quietly and without expectation, for the person who is hardest for you to love today.

A Note for the Grandkids

Grandpa was 19 years old, struggling and homesick, living with a companion who was hard on him — and instead of giving up or fighting back, he started secretly ironing the man's shirts and polishing his shoes. Nobody ever knew. The resentment went away. The respect came. He has been using this trick ever since. If you are ever stuck hating someone you have to be around — try serving them quietly, without telling anyone. See what happens.
