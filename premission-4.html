Part One: From the Journals  ·  Blanding, Utah, 1963

Elo and the Bull

D
Dee Stevens Devil's Punch Bowl  ·  San Juan County, Utah  ·  March, 1963
✦

My best friend that year was Elo Heyman. He was not a member of the Church, but he had qualities that rubbed off on a person whether they intended it or not — powerful athleticism, a stamina that seemed almost inhuman, and an enthusiasm for the wilderness that matched my own. We spent hours together in the canyons and mesas around Blanding, learning the country the way boys learn things they genuinely love: through their feet, their hands, and the occasional near-disaster.

The Devil's Punch Bowl was our favorite location. It sat about five miles east of town through rocky wilderness you had to hike because no roads led there — a small canyon with a twenty-foot rock falls forming a pool at the bottom, a large cave on the west and south sides, and further along the wash a series of crystal-clear pools, some of them over our heads. Along the canyon walls ran the bricked-off cliff dwellings of the ancient Moqui Indians, a thousand years empty, still full of pottery and arrowheads if you knew where to look.

We had two dogs that came everywhere with us. Smokey was mine. Herbert was Elo's. They were fine dogs in most circumstances. This story is about the circumstances they were not fine in.

· · ·

It was March, so the water in the pools was ice water in the technical sense — cold enough to be genuinely dangerous, well below anything a sensible person would consider swimming in. I established my sensibility early by putting my feet in for a few seconds and getting out. I found a rock at the edge of the pool, sat down, and prepared to watch.

Elo, being stark raving crazy from time to time, took off every stitch of his clothing and waded in up to his chest.

He was screaming. He was yapping. He was calling me names with considerable creativity. I was laughing and denying my guilt and then admitting it. This is what friendship looked like in Blanding, Utah in 1963.

Then a huge snort sounded directly above my head.

I looked up. A bull was standing over me blowing steam out of his nostrils, close enough that I could smell him. I rolled sideways off the rock and down the bank of the wash as fast as a person can roll. The bull walked forward, stepping directly over Elo's clothes, and positioned himself between Elo and every stitch he owned.

The situation, assessed
Elo: chest-deep in ice water, naked, turning blue.
His clothes: on the bank, directly behind a bull.
The canyon walls: too steep to climb anywhere except where the bull was standing.
The dogs: nowhere. Absolutely nowhere.
The sky: clouds rolling in, a stiff March wind picking up behind them.

Elo began yelling for me to do something. I tried yelling at the bull. I threw rocks at him. I found a stick and smacked him in a location I will describe only as persuasive. He did not budge. He stood with the patience of an animal that has absolutely nowhere else to be and regarded us both with complete indifference.

The blue day had gone dismal grey. Elo's yelling had taken on a tone of genuine desperation. I stood there with a stick in my hand, having exhausted my full repertoire of bull-removal strategies, feeling rather useless.

Then, from somewhere down the wash, the dogs materialized.

They saw the bull approximately when the bull saw them. What followed was best described as the charge of the light brigade — barking, snorting, howling, and a considerable quantity of dust as the dogs nipped at the bull's heels and the bull kicked and stumbled his way down the wash and out of our lives.

I have never seen anyone get dressed as fast as Elo Heyman did at that moment. He was halfway up the canyon before I registered that he was out of the pool.

· · ·

The bull episode had put us badly behind schedule, and climbing out of the wash we found that the March wind had returned with full conviction. We had not dressed for it. Four miles of open mesa between us and town, straight into the face of that wind.

The walk home
Four miles into a March wind. Walking backwards half the time just to breathe.
Eyes watering so badly they could barely see each other.
Lying down behind stands of sage or juniper when they couldn't go on, which didn't really help.
Then: an abandoned ranch site. A pile of old appliances. The drum of a washing machine, torn apart and left to rust.
They built a lean-to from the washer parts, packed it with old straw, and started a fire. Heaven was never so good as that fire.

They sat twenty minutes getting warm, which was exactly what they needed and also exactly the wrong thing to do in terms of what happened next. Neither of them thought about the smoke. Neither of them considered what a column of smoke rising from an abandoned ranch site might look like to someone passing on the road.

I stood up and my pants had gotten too hot. I looked around.

Down the road: what appeared to be a whole fleet of police cars and fire trucks, roaring toward them at speed.

"Cheese it! The Cops!"

They were over the fence and across the fields the back way before the vehicles had stopped. The fear of prison for starting a fire cleared their heads entirely for the remaining miles home, which was at least one way to solve the cold problem.

"We never had a dull moment, Elo and I. I could write a volume about that one year alone."

Who Elo Heyman was

Elo was not a member of the Church, but I considered him one of the formative friendships of my boyhood. He was a powerful athlete, gifted with stamina that seemed almost superhuman. We hiked together, ran together, and survived each other's bad ideas for the better part of two years. I never forgot him. I wrote about him in my journal fifteen years later as someone who had shaped me — not through any formal teaching, but through the specific education you only get from a friend who will wade naked into ice water just to see what happens.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa was twelve years old when this happened. His friend Elo stripped down and waded into ice water in March, a bull walked over his clothes and stood between him and them, and the two dogs who were supposed to help were nowhere until the exact moment they were finally needed. Then Grandpa and Elo built an illegal fire in an abandoned ranch and ran from what looked like every police car in San Juan County. They made it home. Their mothers were delighted to see them, as mothers always are when their sons return from situations like this smelling of smoke and cattle.
