Part One: From the Journals  ·  Blanding, Utah

Stephen Burtenshaw's Grandfather

D
Dee Stevens Blanding, Utah  ·  1962–1963
✦

That same year there was a boy in Blanding named Stephen Burtenshaw who was a year older than me. He was the kind of young man you notice — not because he sought attention, but because he carried himself with a steadiness that most boys his age had not yet found. He was dedicated to his priesthood duties, friendly without effort, and a natural leader in the way that has nothing to do with rank or position. Much of how I thought about working on my own character during those two years in Blanding was shaped by watching Steve.

The Burtenshaws lived next door to his grandparents, who were old and in poor health. The proximity meant Steve was close to his grandfather's last years in a way that most boys are not close to that kind of thing — close enough to observe it, close enough to be changed by it.

· · ·

One evening there was a fireside for the youth of the ward. Steve stood up to bear his testimony, which was not unusual — he bore it regularly, and when he did it meant something. But that evening what he had to say was different from the ordinary testimony of a young man who had been raised in the Church and believed what he had been taught.

He told us about his grandfather.

His grandfather had been lying in bed one night, quite sick. And then something happened that the old man afterward struggled to put into words. He looked down and saw his own body, still in the bed. His spirit had left it. He was outside himself, looking at the form he had occupied his whole life, lying there — and he understood in that moment what was happening and what it would mean if it continued.

As Stephen Burtenshaw told it  ·  A youth fireside  ·  Blanding, Utah
He looked down and saw his body still in the bed.
He feared he was not ready to pass on.
He was allowed to return — through much pain and sorrow.
He woke the next morning, alive.

He was not ready. That was the thing he knew with certainty, looking down at himself. He was not ready. And somehow — in a way he could not fully account for, in a way that cost him real suffering to accomplish — he was allowed to return. He came back into his body through what he described as considerable pain and sorrow. He woke up the next morning alive, in his bed, in his body, with one more day he had not expected to have.

Steve told this story at a youth fireside in Blanding, Utah, in front of a room full of twelve and thirteen year olds, and the room went still in the way rooms go still when something true has been said in them.

· · ·

I was extremely touched by it. That is the phrase I used in my journal, written not long after: extremely touched. It is not a sophisticated formulation, but it is an accurate one. The story reached something in me that the ordinary Sunday lessons had not quite reached — not because those lessons were wrong, but because this was different. This was a boy I respected, reporting something his grandfather had actually experienced, in a room where the Spirit confirmed that the report was true.

What it did to a twelve-year-old boy
"I was extremely touched by this event. I often returned to it during testimony meetings with my friends."

I returned to it often. During testimony meetings in the years that followed, when I was trying to find something real to say — something that was mine and not just borrowed from a lesson manual — I would think about Stephen Burtenshaw standing at a fireside in Blanding, and about an old man who looked down at his own body and chose to come back through the pain because he was not finished yet.

"The doctrine of the spirit and the body is one thing when you read it. It is another thing when someone who has been to the edge of that boundary stands up in a room and tells you what he saw from there."

I have thought about that grandfather many times. I do not know his name — Steve did not say it, or if he did I have not retained it. What I retained was the image: a man outside his own body, looking at himself, knowing he was not ready, and finding his way back.

There is a version of that story in my own life, decades later — a night in an ICU when things were going wrong and the outcome was not certain. I thought about Stephen Burtenshaw's grandfather then too. I thought: he was not ready, and he was allowed more time. I asked for the same. The request was granted, as it turned out. I did not see my body from outside it. But I understood, in a way I had first understood at twelve years old in a room in Blanding, that the boundary is real and that what lies on the other side of it is not nothing.

Steve taught me that. His grandfather showed him. He brought it back and shared it at a fireside, the way faithful people share things that matter — openly, without performance, because the truth of it is too large to keep to yourself.

A Note for the Grandkids In Blanding, when Grandpa was twelve, there was a boy a year older named Stephen Burtenshaw who he admired and tried to pattern himself after. One evening at a youth fireside, Steve stood up and told a story about his grandfather, who had been very sick one night and whose spirit had left his body. The grandfather looked down and saw himself in the bed, understood what was happening, felt he was not ready to go, and was allowed to return — through real pain — and woke up alive the next morning. Grandpa wrote in his journal that he was extremely touched by it. He returned to the story many times during testimony meetings for the rest of his life. Decades later, in a hospital, when things were uncertain for him too, he thought about that grandfather again. He asked for more time. He got it.
