A Life of Tender Mercies  ·  Dee Stevens
Part Six: Building a Life  ·  Chapter Thirty-Nine

Callings

D
Dee Stevens West Valley, Pleasant Grove & Lindon, Utah  ·  1985–2018
✦

Not every chapter of a faithful life is dramatic. Most of the work is quiet — showing up on a Sunday morning, sitting in a meeting, preparing a lesson nobody asked you to prepare, visiting someone who probably wasn't expecting you. The callings that shaped me most were not the ones that made good stories. They were the ones that made me better.

I want to write about some of them, because they deserve to be in the record even if the record is not particularly exciting.

· · ·

While I was completing my schooling at BYU, I was set apart as the High Priest Group Leader in our Pleasant Grove ward. There were fifty brothers on the rolls — a large group. I felt immediately that I was in over my head, which was remedied when I called Pete Johnson and Johnnie O'Connor as my assistants. Both of them knew more about the calling than I did. I had the good sense to know that and the better sense to listen to them. They became my tutors, and working alongside them was one of the genuine pleasures of those years.

Johnnie O'Connor was a particular man. He had a strong testimony and a certainty about how to share it, and he shared it every fast Sunday without fail — not out of habit, but out of conviction. The whole group came to count on it. He was, as I would put it, an anchor of the meeting.

He died of a heart condition. His loss was felt in a way that is difficult to quantify — he had been so thoroughly present for so long that his absence was its own kind of presence.

Pleasant Grove Ward  ·  First fast Sunday after Johnnie's passing
The meeting went on as usual. Testimonies were borne, one after another.
Then there was a pause — an extended silence right where Brother O'Connor would have stood.
Nobody moved to fill it. Nobody needed to explain why.

I have been in many testimony meetings over many decades. I have never been in one quite like that one. The silence said everything that words would have diluted.

· · ·

After two years as High Priest Group Leader I was released and called as the ward clerk under Bishop Kevin Stoker. His counselors were David Adkins and Bradley Lamb — good men who took their responsibilities seriously and made it easy to do the same. It was during this time that the Church transitioned from paper records to a computer system, which I welcomed without reservation. Printing became simple. Communication with headquarters became faster. The work that had once required filing cabinets and careful handwriting became something you could manage in a fraction of the time.

The choir director who wasn't

Early in that period, a counselor had called and asked if Dee would mind substituting as choir director for ward conference — just until they found someone. He agreed. Ward conference came and went. Nobody was called. He kept showing up. He led the choir through two full years as a substitute.

One day in a bishopric meeting, Bishop Stoker looked up from his desk and asked: "Who is our choir director, anyway?" Bro. Adkins, sitting next to Dee, began pointing at him under the table, trying to spare the bishop some embarrassment. The bishop got the message and said, "Oh, I know Bro. Stevens leads the choir — but who is actually the choir director?" The answer was: nobody. They released him and called someone else. He took it in stride.

From clerk I was called to be the Sunday School president. We were having trouble getting teachers to show up — even the Gospel Doctrine teacher seemed to be out of town a great deal. I solved this by dividing the classes into three groups and assigning each of us in the presidency to supervise a different group, with standing instructions to be prepared to teach any class we oversaw if the teacher was absent. My group was the two adult classes. I loved those discussions — the Book of Mormon with a room full of adults who had lived long enough to have something to say about it.

· · ·

But the calling I think about most from those years is not one I held officially. It is the one that began next door.

We were living in West Valley at the time, and I was teaching the Blazer B class in Primary — boys about ten or eleven years old, the kind of energy level that makes a Sunday lesson feel like herding cats. Our next-door neighbor was not a member in good standing. He was a capable and intelligent man, somewhat imposing in his manner, with one glass eye that could produce a rather alarming effect when he chose to deploy it. I concluded he might be an asset.

I stopped by one afternoon and asked if he would consider serving as my assistant scout leader. He said he would think about it.

West Valley Ward  ·  Blazer B class
The following Sunday, the neighbor walked in and sat with the class.
The boys began to act out as they always did. He stood up, fixed them with his glass eye, and said: "We are here in church to learn the gospel, and you all better shut up and listen to the lesson."
Not one boy made a sound after that. Not once, for the rest of the year.

The class environment that resulted was, as you might imagine, excellent. The lessons could breathe. The Spirit had room to come in and stay. One Sunday the lesson was on the Holy Ghost, and at the close of it I felt a strong impression to bear my testimony. When the prayer was done, my neighbor came and found me. He said the lessons had felt, more and more, as though they were specifically meant for him. He thanked me for teaching. He said he had decided to turn over a new leaf and bring his family back to church.

He went home and told his wife and children. They came to church as a family. Not long after that he was called as the ward clerk.

"I consider it a singularly great experience in my life — witnessing a person hear the doctrine and decide to change. The glass eye helped. But it was the Holy Ghost who did the actual work."

· · ·

I have held callings in nearly every ward I have ever lived in. Choir director, more times than I can count. Sunday School teacher. Elders Quorum. High Priest Group Leader. Ward clerk. Sunday School president. Family history consultant. Temple and Family History Leader. Young Men's president during the years Travis, Jared, and Michael received their mission calls. Stake missionary, twice.

None of these positions appear on any professional record. None of them came with compensation or a title anyone outside the ward would recognize. Most of them involved showing up, doing the unglamorous thing, and trusting that the Lord would use the effort for something even if the results were not immediately visible.

Occasionally the results were visible. The neighbor with the glass eye became the ward clerk. The boy who didn't want to go on a mission went. The investigator who had been holding out for years finally accepted baptism. The family history group that did nothing found itself reporting thirty-two families visited the following month.

But mostly the results were not visible. Mostly you just showed up and taught the lesson and visited the brother who wasn't coming and tried to keep the choir on pitch and printed the records and updated the database and drove home wondering if any of it had mattered.

I believe it did. I believe the Lord sees the unglamorous things more clearly than we do, and accounts for them more carefully than we would expect. I have no evidence for this beyond seventy-four years of watching what happens when people show up faithfully to the quiet work — and what happens when they don't.

The quiet work matters. It always has. It always will.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa held callings in every ward he ever lived in, for his entire adult life — choir director, Sunday School teacher, ward clerk, High Priest Group Leader, Sunday School president, family history leader, and many more. Most of it was quiet and unglamorous. Two stories stand out. One: his assistant in the High Priest Group, a man named Johnnie O'Connor, died unexpectedly. The first fast Sunday afterward, the testimony meeting went on as usual — and then there was a long silence right where Johnnie always stood. Nobody moved to fill it. The other: in West Valley he asked his inactive neighbor, who had a glass eye, to assist with the Blazer B scouts. The neighbor came to class, fixed the boys with his glass eye, told them to be quiet and learn the gospel, and they were — for the rest of the year. The Spirit came into those lessons. The neighbor went home afterward and told his family they were coming back to church. He was eventually called as the ward clerk. Grandpa says the Holy Ghost did the actual work. The glass eye was just the opening.
