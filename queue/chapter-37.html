Part Six: Building a Life  ·  Chapter Thirty-Seven

Ben's Roof

D
Dee Stevens St. George, Utah  ·  Early 1990s
✦

Ben was fearless. I want to say that first, because it is the context for everything else in this story. He played catcher in baseball and he was fearless there too — took the foul tips and the collisions at the plate without flinching. He was the kind of boy who approached the world at a run and trusted that things would work out, and mostly they did.

Until the afternoon he stumbled off our roof.

We were living in St. George at the time — our five-bedroom home, the one Aunt Janeen and Uncle Dale had helped us find after we fasted for it — and Ben had been up on the roof for some reason that I don't remember now and probably didn't fully understand at the time either. What I know is that he stumbled and fell headlong onto the driveway below. It was about a fifteen-foot drop.

One of his eyes came out of its socket.

He also had his gum caught in his throat. He was unconscious and choking.

The Lord had arranged our neighborhood thoughtfully. Our neighbor was a policeman, and he reached Ben before the ambulance arrived. He was able to get the gum out so Ben could breathe. That was the first thing that had to happen, and it happened because of where we lived and who lived next to us.

Kathy went with Ben in the helicopter to Primary Children's Hospital in Salt Lake. Another trusted neighbor met us there. We gathered around Ben and administered to him — laid our hands on his head and promised him by the Spirit that he would be healed. I want to be clear about that word: healed. Not that he would improve, not that we hoped for the best. We promised him he would be healed.

· · ·

The surgeons at Primary Children's re-inserted his eye successfully. He recovered. And before he came home — before he was released from the hospital — he passed the sacrament at a sacrament meeting held for patients and families on the ward.

Primary Children's Hospital  ·  Salt Lake City

"I consider it a miracle that he was restored to perfect health."

That detail — the sacrament — is the one I keep coming back to. He had fallen off a roof. His eye had come out of his head. He had been airlifted to Salt Lake and operated on. And before he came home he was on his feet in a hospital corridor passing the sacrament to patients in their beds. That is the kind of boy he was. That is the kind of young man he became.

· · ·

Ben went on to become deeply invested in scouting as he grew older — not just as a participant but as a leader. Well into his twenties he was serving as an assistant scoutmaster in our troop in Pleasant Grove, working with boys who reminded him of himself at that age. Fearless. Running at the world. Trusting things would work out.

"He was restored to perfect health. The blessing promised it by the Spirit. The Lord kept His word, as He always does when the blessing is given by the Spirit and not by wishful thinking."

What the neighbor understood

He did not know Ben. He was simply the man who lived next door, who happened to be a policeman, who happened to be home that afternoon. By the time the ambulance arrived the airway was clear and Ben was breathing. The neighbor asked for nothing. He went home afterward and that was that.

Dee has thought many times about how differently that afternoon could have gone if a different person had lived in that house. He has concluded the Lord does not leave that kind of thing to chance.

Ben is gone now — taken too soon, as I have written elsewhere in these pages. But I keep this story alongside that one because they are both true and both need to be part of the record. He survived a fifteen-foot fall onto a driveway and was restored to perfect health. He passed the sacrament in a hospital corridor. He led scouts in Pleasant Grove into his twenties. He had a full life and he lived it the way he had always approached things — at a run, trusting it would work out.

It did. Until it didn't. But that is a different chapter.

This one is about the afternoon in St. George when it did.

A Note for the Grandkids Your Uncle Ben was fearless his whole life. In St. George, when he was a teenager, he stumbled off the roof of the family home — about fifteen feet — and landed headlong on the driveway. One of his eyes came out of its socket. He was unconscious with gum in his throat. The neighbor was a policeman and got to him before the ambulance, cleared his airway so he could breathe. Kathy flew with him by helicopter to Primary Children's Hospital in Salt Lake. The surgeons put his eye back in. Before he came home, he passed the sacrament at a sacrament meeting held on the hospital ward. He recovered completely. Grandpa gave him a blessing that promised by the Spirit he would be healed. The Lord kept the promise. Ben became an assistant scoutmaster in Pleasant Grove and served boys who reminded him of himself. He had a full life, lived at a run. Grandpa keeps this story as carefully as all the others.
