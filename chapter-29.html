Part Five: Letting Go  ·  Chapter Twenty-Nine

The Boy With the Fork

D
Dee Stevens Pleasant Grove, Utah  ·  2004–2017
✦

I had always heard that teaching middle schoolers was the worst job anyone could voluntarily take on. The general theory seemed to be that twelve and thirteen year olds are too old to be managed like children and too young to be reasoned with like adults, and that the years in between are a kind of pedagogical no-man's land where even experienced teachers lose their nerve.

I went in anyway. And I want to tell you what I found there, because it surprised me — and because I think it says something about what happens when the Lord prepares a person for a job over the course of thirty years without telling them that is what He is doing.

My worst habit as a teacher, according to the conventional wisdom, was that I treated my students too much like my own kids. I had raised eight boys. I had coached boys in nearly every sport. I had trained hundreds of young people in the grocery store industry — teenagers who were unreliable and distracted and fully capable of rising to an expectation if you held it high enough and meant it. So when I walked into a middle school classroom at fifty-four years old, these were not strangers to me. They were just more teenagers. Difficult ones, sometimes. But mine.

What nine years at Dixon Middle School looked like

Social studies — geography, history, civics. Writing essays and thesis statements, because that is what his BYU professors told him to teach. An honors US History class for the top eighth graders, developed at the principal's request. A study class after school for students who were struggling. Department chair, twice. New teachers to mentor. Students who came back from Provo High to say he had taught them to write. And the harder ones — the ones other teachers had given up on — parked next to his desk where he could keep an eye on them.

· · ·

There was one student I want to tell you about specifically, because his story is the one that stayed with me longest, and because I believe the Lord put him in my classroom for a reason that had nothing to do with social studies.

He was a bully. That is the plain word for it, and I will use it, because softening it would misrepresent what the other students had been living with. He picked on smaller and younger kids every day. He had been kicked off the school buses. He had been suspended more times than anyone could easily count. He was the kind of student that schools move around from classroom to classroom hoping someone will eventually find the right approach, and mostly what they find is that nobody does.

Then one day in the cafeteria he grabbed a plastic fork and drove it into the neck of another student with enough force that it stuck. The school nurse had to remove it. He was suspended again — two weeks this time — and when the suspension was over, the decision was made that he should come to my class.

Dee put him in the desk right next to his own. Close enough to helicopter, he said, if it came to that.

I sat him next to my desk, as close as I could get him, and I treated him the same way I treated everyone else. I gave him real work and expected him to do it. I did not manage him from a distance or announce to the class that he was a problem to be monitored. He was a student. He had an assignment. I wanted to see what he would do with it.

He began to succeed.

Not overnight. Not without setbacks. But steadily, in the way that students succeed when they discover that the work is real and the teacher is paying attention and neither of those things is going to change. He began to do his assignments. He began to engage. During an important group project he did not merely participate — he took charge of his group, kept them on task, and led them through a presentation that was good enough that I invited the principal in to watch.

The principal came. He watched. In my next formal evaluation he specifically mentioned this boy — the changes he had seen, the student he had apparently become. I accepted the compliment and said thank you and did not explain that I was not entirely sure what I had done to deserve it.

· · ·

I don't know what specifically turned the corner for him. I have thought about it many times and I cannot identify a single lesson or conversation or moment that changed things. What I did was show up, treat him like someone capable of better, and refuse to be surprised when he was. Whether that is a teaching philosophy or just stubbornness dressed up in different clothes, I leave for others to decide.

What I know is that he became loyal to me in a way I did not expect and did not entirely know what to do with. He was protective of the classroom in the way that converted students sometimes are — if he found other kids slacking or being disruptive he would turn on them with an intensity that was, at minimum, effective. He had decided this was a place worth taking seriously. He was not going to let anyone waste it.

One morning he found me outside putting up the school flag and told me I was his favorite teacher. Then he said he wanted to bring me some brownies.

I explained I was diabetic and could not eat brownies.

The next morning he came back with a brown bag. Inside it was a stalk of celery.

"He showed up out front while I was putting up the flag with a brown bag in hand. He had brought me a stalk of celery. I was touched."

He gave us no more trouble the rest of that year. When he moved on to high school, I wrote a letter to his mother telling her what I had seen in her son — that he had become, without question, my most improved student. That the change in him had been one of the genuinely remarkable things I had witnessed in nine years of teaching.

She sent the email back unread. Declined to open it.

I have thought about that too. About what it means to be the mother of a boy like that — what years of phone calls and suspensions and bad news must do to a person, how many times she must have been told about what her son had done before anyone ever thought to tell her about what he could do. I do not judge her. I hope the letter sits somewhere she might find it again someday, on a day when she is ready to read it.

"I don't know what I did for that boy. I know what he did for me. He reminded me why I had gone back to school at fifty-two years old and sat in classrooms full of people young enough to be my grandchildren. It was for him. It was always for him."

· · ·

I taught at Dixon until I retired in 2017. In my final years there I developed over a hundred and forty lesson and assessment plans — everything broadcast to a wall screen through an Apple TV so the students could follow along, engaged in a way that chalk and a blackboard never quite managed. Our principal secured new electronic equipment for the whole school, and I threw myself into it. You are never too old to learn a new way to do the thing you love.

I was often impatient for the new school year to begin. I would sit in August and feel September approaching the way I felt things when I was young — with an eagerness I had not expected at my age, in a profession I had not found until most people are thinking about leaving theirs.

I am grateful every day that the dialysis took me out of the grocery business. I am grateful for the bruises that opened the door at BYU. I am grateful for the grant that arrived on the last possible day. I am grateful for the four years in hard rooms with students in state custody, who taught me more than I taught them. All of it was preparation. The Lord had been building a teacher for thirty years. He knew something I did not: that there was a boy in a middle school in Pleasant Grove who was going to need someone to sit him down next to a desk and refuse to be surprised by him.

He brought me celery.

I was touched.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa taught middle school social studies for nine years. One of his students was a serious bully — suspended repeatedly, eventually kicked off the school buses — who one day stabbed another student in the neck with a plastic fork hard enough that it had to be removed by the school nurse. The school sent him to Grandpa's class. Grandpa put him in the desk right next to his own and treated him like any other student. The boy turned around. He took charge of a group project. He became protective of the classroom. He told Grandpa he was his favorite teacher and wanted to bring him brownies. Grandpa explained he was diabetic. The next morning the boy showed up with a stalk of celery in a brown bag. Grandpa called him his most improved student ever. The boy's mother sent the letter back unread. Grandpa still thinks about her.
