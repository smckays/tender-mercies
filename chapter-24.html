A Life of Tender Mercies  ·  Dee Stevens
Part Four: Kathy  ·  Chapter Twenty-Four

Ben

D
Dee Stevens St. George, Utah  ·  Mid-1990s
✦

Benjamin was Kathy's third son and her baby — the one she had carried the special bond with that mothers sometimes carry with their youngest. He was fearless in the way that certain boys are fearless: not reckless, exactly, but constitutionally unbothered by the things that frighten more cautious people. He played catcher in baseball without flinching. He climbed things. He threw himself into whatever was in front of him with complete commitment.

That quality served him well in most areas of his life. It almost killed him twice.

· · ·

The first time he nearly died was on a summer afternoon in St. George when he went up on the roof of our house to do something — I no longer remember what — and stumbled and fell. Fifteen feet, straight down onto the driveway below. Head first.

What the fall did
He hit headlong onto the concrete driveway from fifteen feet up.
One of his eyes actually came out of its socket.
He also got gum caught in his throat — he was unconscious and choking simultaneously.
Our neighbor, a policeman, arrived before the ambulance. He cleared the gum from Ben's throat so he could breathe.
Kathy and Ben were taken by helicopter to Primary Children's Hospital in Salt Lake City.

I want to pause here and ask you to sit with what I just described. A fifteen-foot fall onto concrete. An eye out of its socket. A boy choking unconscious on the driveway. In the space of a few minutes, a policeman neighbor who happened to be home, a helicopter, and a hospital three hundred miles away.

The Lord had stationed people and resources around that boy.

At the hospital another trusted neighbor met us. Together we administered to Ben — laid our hands on his head and promised him by the Spirit that he would be healed. It was not a hope. It was a promise. You know the difference when you feel it.

What happened at Primary Children's Hospital
They re-inserted his eye successfully.
Before he came home, Ben passed the sacrament at a hospital sacrament meeting.
He was restored to perfect health.

He passed the sacrament. In a hospital meeting. Before coming home from a helicopter evacuation for a fall that should have killed him. That detail tells you everything you need to know about Benjamin — about his faith, about his character, about what kind of young man he was becoming even through the things that tried to stop him.

· · ·

I consider it a miracle. Not in the vague sense in which people sometimes use that word — as a synonym for something fortunate or coincidental — but in the full sense. A specific intervention by a God who was paying specific attention to a specific boy on a specific afternoon in St. George, Utah.

Kathy was especially shaken by this one. Ben was her baby and they had always had a particular bond, and the image of him on that driveway was not something she could put away easily. She carried the fear of losing him for some time afterward — a tender and very human response to watching your child survive something that should not have been survivable.

"The Lord kept Ben. Not because Ben was perfect — none of us are — but because He had something for Ben to do yet. That is the only explanation I have, and I believe it completely."

· · ·

Ben grew up to become deeply invested in Boy Scouts — not just as a participant but as a leader, serving as an assistant scoutmaster well into his twenties, taking boys on high adventure camps and pouring into them the same fearless energy that had characterized his own growing up. He had been shaped by what he survived, and what he survived had made him into someone who could guide others through hard things without flinching.

What happened later

In Pleasant Grove, years after St. George, Ben suffered what appeared to be a heart episode. He had gone outside to take some garbage while the family was celebrating a birthday. When he didn't return, a search found his body in the yard. He was Kathy's baby from her first marriage, and they had always had their special bond. His passing was among the hardest things she ever bore.

I have placed that detail here gently and without elaboration, because it belongs in the record and because you deserve to know it — but it is not the chapter that holds his ending. This chapter holds his survival, his healing, his eye put back, his passing the sacrament in a hospital gown before the helicopter brought him home.

That is Benjamin. Fearless. Faithful. The boy the Lord kept, at least for a while, with a policeman neighbor and a promise laid on his head by his father's hands.

We loved him. We still do.

A Note for the Grandkids Ben was Grandma Kathy's youngest son — your Uncle Ben. He fell fifteen feet off a roof in St. George and landed headlong on the driveway. His eye came out of its socket. A neighbor who happened to be a policeman kept him breathing until the ambulance came. A helicopter took him to Primary Children's Hospital. Grandpa and a neighbor gave him a priesthood blessing and promised him he would be healed. Before he came home, Ben passed the sacrament at a hospital meeting. His eye was put back. He was restored to perfect health. He grew up to be a Scout leader who poured himself into other boys the same way people had poured themselves into him. Grandpa considers what happened on that driveway a full and specific miracle. He always will.
