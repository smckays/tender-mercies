Part Two: Love, Loss, and Starting Over  ·  Chapter Fourteen

The Girl Who Flew Out the Window

D
Dee Stevens Highway 95, White Bird Pass, Idaho  ·  Summer, 1975
❧

I want to tell you something that happened on a mountain highway in Idaho in the summer of 1975. I am going to tell it to you plainly and exactly as it occurred, and I understand if you find it difficult to believe. I found it difficult to believe myself, and I was there.

My daughter Keri Lyn was not quite two years old. She was a bright, sweet little girl — the kind of child who fills a room just by being in it. She had already survived more than any baby should have to: a mysterious illness in her first year that had robbed her of her ability to walk and talk, a long stay at Primary Children's Hospital, and a slow, hard-won recovery that her doctors couldn't fully explain. We knew she was a child watched over by someone other than us.

That summer we had decided to move to Spokane, Washington — a fresh start, new city, new ward, a chance to put some distance between ourselves and the difficulties that had been accumulating back in Provo. We packed everything we owned into a small U-Haul trailer and pointed the car north.

What we had not discovered, when we hooked up the trailer, was that the wiring between our car and the trailer was faulty. The trailer had no working brakes.

We found this out coming down White Bird Pass.

· · ·

If you have never driven White Bird Pass in Idaho, it is a long steep descent through mountain switchbacks — the kind of road that demands your full attention and your full brakes. When I hit the brakes and felt the trailer begin to sway, I knew immediately we were in trouble.

I did everything I could think of. I gripped the wheel. I tried to correct. But we were moving too fast, and with each correction the trailer swung wider, swerving back and forth behind us in longer and more violent arcs. The last time it swung, it lifted the rear wheels of our car clean off the pavement and dragged us toward the edge of the road.

I said one sentence out loud. I don't remember deciding to say it. It came from somewhere beneath thought:

"Father, save us."

Almost immediately — not gradually, not after a long struggle, but almost immediately — the trailer came into line. We were back under control. We pulled off the road and sat there in the sudden stillness, hearts hammering, and gave thanks.

We thought that was the story. We were wrong.

· · ·

Some miles later we had come down out of the mountains and were coasting along a quieter stretch of highway. In those days seatbelts were not the law and were rarely used. Linda had fallen asleep in the back seat. Keri Lyn was standing on the front seat beside me — this was simply how small children rode in cars in 1975, pressed against their parents, watching the road go by.

We had the windows down. It was a beautiful summer afternoon and the cool air coming through the mountains felt like a reward after the terror of the pass. Keri was enjoying it — her hair moving in the wind, her small face turned toward the open window.

I had to swerve. Something on the road — I don't remember what, just an obstacle, a small correction. The kind of thing you do a hundred times on any long drive without consequence.

In the blink of an eye, Keri Lyn was gone.

Her little body went out the passenger window head first.
In my mind I saw her crushed beneath the wheels of the trailer.
I yelled to Linda that her baby was gone.
She barely had enough time to open her eyes and sit up —
when back through the window came Keri Lyn,
landing safely on the seat.
Four seconds. Maybe five. Head first out. Head first back in.
A word from Dee

I know when you read this you will doubt my words and think I must have been mistaken. But I testify to you in solemnity — she went out the window head first and came back in head first, with barely four or five seconds between. Linda and I are both still alive as I write this, and we will both bear witness to this miracle. This is not a story I have embellished over the years. It is exactly what happened.

We stopped the car. We held our daughter. We prayed.

Then we drove on to Spokane, shaking and grateful and certain of one thing: this child was not ours to keep or to lose. She was being held by hands larger than ours.

· · ·

Keri Lyn lived another nine years after that afternoon on the Idaho highway. They were not easy years — she faced surgeries, learning struggles, a childhood marked by one difficulty after another. She died in 1984 at the age of ten, from bone marrow cancer, while on a family trip to Hawaii.

I have written about her passing elsewhere in these pages. It deserves its own chapter, and it will get one. But I wanted to tell you this story first — the window story — because I think it is the key to understanding everything that came after.

"We were not in charge of Keri Lyn. We never had been. She was watched over from somewhere above our pay grade, on a timeline we were not shown."

Parents are not supposed to outlive their children. When it happens, the grief is a particular kind — bottomless in a way that other grief is not, because it violates something fundamental about the order of things. I won't pretend I didn't feel that. I did.

But I had stood on a mountain highway and said Father, save us and been answered. I had watched my daughter fly out a window and come back. I had held her afterward and felt, as clearly as I have ever felt anything, that she was not mine to protect — that she was on loan to us, watched over by someone who loved her more completely than I was capable of.

When she died, the Spirit bore witness to me that taking her home was an act of mercy. She had suffered so much in her short life. She would have suffered more. I didn't receive that witness because I was especially righteous or because I had earned it. I received it because the Lord knew I needed it, and because He had been preparing me for it since that afternoon on the Idaho highway — teaching me, one miracle at a time, that He was paying attention.

That is what I want you to know about your great-aunt Keri Lyn. She was bright and sweet and full of life. She was watched over every day she was here. And she was loved — by her father, completely and without reservation — every single one of those days.

Even the ones after she was gone.

A Note for the Grandkids Keri Lyn was your grandfather's firstborn child. She died before most of your parents were born, so very few of you ever had the chance to know her. But she is real, and she is part of your family, and she is buried in West Jordan, Utah. Grandpa wants you to know her story. This is the first chapter of it — the afternoon on a highway in Idaho when she flew out a window and came back. He has never forgotten that afternoon. He never will.
