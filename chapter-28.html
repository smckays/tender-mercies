Part Five: Letting Go  ·  Chapter Twenty-Eight

Back to School at Fifty-Four

D
Dee Stevens Pleasant Grove, Utah  ·  2000–2004
✦

BYU offered me a plan to finish my degree from where I had left off in 1972. The state of Utah would cover the first year. I said yes before the counselor had finished the sentence.

I want to be clear about what I was saying yes to. I was in my early fifties, recovering from a kidney transplant, living in Pleasant Grove with Kathy and a house that had seen better days. I had spent my career in grocery management — thirty years of early mornings and store directors and produce schedules. I had never stood in front of a classroom. I did not know if I could do it. What I knew was that I could not work, that the dialysis years had taken something out of me, and that the Lord had apparently arranged for a door to open. Walking through it seemed like the only honest response.

So I went back to school. I was the oldest student in nearly every class. This did not embarrass me the way some people might expect. I had learned enough by then to know that being the oldest person in the room is not the same as being the least capable one, and that the students who most annoyed their younger classmates — the ones who had read everything, who asked too many questions, who would not let a wrong answer stand — were often the ones who actually knew something.

I tried not to be too annoying. I am not sure I always succeeded.

· · ·

The program moved well. The state grant covered the first year. Scholarships carried me through most of the rest. I was working toward a secondary teaching certificate in social studies — a subject I had always loved, the kind of history and civics and geography that explains why the world is shaped the way it is. Kathy kept us going through those years the way she kept us going through everything: steadily, without complaint, with faith that the plan was sound even when the money was thin.

And then we hit the final semester.

I needed one more semester of coursework to complete my certification. The scholarships were gone. We had no income. The tuition and books payment was due, and we had nowhere to go for it. Kathy and I prayed about it the way we had learned to pray about impossible things — specifically, earnestly, without pretense. We asked for help. Then we waited.

On the last day before payment was due, a check arrived in the mail.

Pleasant Grove, Utah  ·  Final semester, 2004
"It was from the social studies department — a grant. For the exact amount we needed. Not close to it. The exact amount, tuition and books. We had not applied for it. We had not known it existed. We had only asked the Lord."

I graduated in August of 2004 with a secondary teaching license. I had not paid a cent for my bachelor's degree. The state covered the first year, scholarships covered the years after that, and when the money ran out entirely the Lord covered the last semester Himself.

I was fifty-three years old. I was a teacher.

· · ·

My first assignment was at a school for students who were not quite ready for the mainstream — kids who had fallen behind, or fallen apart, or simply never been given a fair shot at either. I liked them immediately. There is something clarifying about teaching students who have reason not to trust you, because you cannot coast on charm or authority. You have to show up prepared, every day, and demonstrate through what you do rather than what you say that you are worth their time. I found I was willing to do that. I found I was good at it.

My second assignment took me further — to a school for students in state custody, young people whose family situations had put them in the care of the state of Utah. The school operated out of donated rooms at a mental health facility and a detention center, and the teachers I worked alongside were some of the most skilled and patient educators I have ever met. They had years of experience I did not have. They were generous with it. I learned more in those four years than I had learned in the classroom at BYU.

What those four years taught him

That a student who has every reason to shut down will sometimes choose not to, if you give them real work and treat them like someone capable of doing it. That progress in a young person is rarely linear and almost always worth celebrating anyway. That the teachers who last in hard rooms are the ones who find the students interesting — not as problems to solve but as people to know. Dee found them interesting. He always had.

At the end of four years the facility lost its funding and closed. On the same day — the same day the doors shut — a social studies teacher at a middle school in the district was accepted into a graduate program. His leaving created a vacancy. The director of Youth in Custody arranged for me to fill it.

I have stopped being surprised when the Lord does things like this. He tends to close one door and open another so quickly that if you blink you miss the transition. The trick is not to spend too long mourning what closed.

· · ·

I walked into a middle school classroom for the first time at fifty-four years old, which is not the conventional age at which a person begins a teaching career. My students were twelve and thirteen. I was old enough to be their grandfather. This gap, which might have been a problem, turned out to be mostly an advantage.

What the classroom became
Social studies. Geography, history, civics — the story of how the world got to be this way.
140 lesson and assessment plans, eventually. Broadcast to a wall screen through an Apple TV. Students more engaged than he had expected.
New teachers to mentor. A department chair role, twice. The work of keeping the subject alive for twelve-year-olds who had not yet decided if it mattered.
Nine years. He was often impatient for the new school year to begin.

I loved my students. That is the plain truth of it. I loved social studies and I loved teaching it, and when those two things are true the students tend to feel it. Not all of them — there are always students you cannot reach, and carrying that is part of the job. But enough of them. Enough that I looked forward to September the way I had looked forward to things when I was young, with the particular eagerness of someone who knows the work matters and cannot wait to get back to it.

"I was a fifty-four-year-old first-year teacher. I was the oldest person in nearly every room I walked into. I had never been happier in my professional life."

I think about the chain of events that put me there. The dialysis that took me out of grocery management. The night in the ICU that left me covered in bruises. The state official who stared at those bruises and picked up the phone. The counselor at BYU who reviewed my 1972 record and saw a degree worth finishing. The grant that arrived on the last day before the money was due. Each piece was necessary. None of it was accidental.

The Lord had been building a teacher for thirty years. I had not known that was what He was doing. I was busy surviving, managing, raising sons, watching Kathy hold everything together. But He was keeping track of the larger arc, the way He always does, and when the time came He had everything in place.

He just needed me to pull up my shirt.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa went back to college in his early fifties — old enough to be the grandfather of most of his classmates — and earned a teaching degree in 2004. The state of Utah covered his first year. Scholarships covered most of the rest. When the money ran out entirely in his last semester, a grant arrived in the mail on the final day before payment was due, for the exact amount needed. He had not applied for it. He did not know it existed. He and Grandma Kathy had only prayed. He taught middle school social studies for nine years after that. He was often impatient for September to come. Some people find out what they are meant to do early. Grandpa found out at fifty-four. It turned out to be worth the wait.
