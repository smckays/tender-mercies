Part One: From the Journals  ·  Blanding, Utah

Decker Noonan

D
Dee Stevens Blanding, Utah  ·  Fall 1962
✦

That fall something happened that changed the way I understood the world.

There had been a football game at San Juan High School on an afternoon in the fall of 1962. A seventh grader named Decker Noonan was working as the team's trainer that year — his job was to be useful, to hand out towels and tape and water, to take care of the equipment. He was not a member of our church but he was one of those boys that every small town has: well loved, full of energy, the kind of person who makes a place feel more alive just by being in it.

A sudden cloudburst came over the town. Decker was sent out onto the field to collect the team's equipment.

As he started across the field, a bolt of lightning struck the goalpost, glanced off, and pierced directly through his head.

His body was thrown approximately fifty feet across the field. He was left with a large hole through his skull.

San Juan High School football field  ·  Blanding, Utah  ·  Fall 1962
A cloudburst. A goalpost. A bolt of lightning that glanced and found him.
Jeff Perkins was on the field with him. He was thrown several feet by the same strike.
The entire town of Blanding turned out to mourn.

The entire town turned out. That is not a figure of speech — in a place the size of Blanding, a loss like Decker Noonan's was not an abstraction. Everyone knew him. Everyone felt it.

Most of the scouts in my troop were the same age as Decker. We had just come through the initiation on Elk Mountain, just earned our blue ribbon at Sand Island, just started to feel like we knew what we were about. We were eleven and twelve years old and we thought we understood the general shape of things. Then Decker was sent out onto a football field on an ordinary fall afternoon and a bolt of lightning found him, and we understood differently.

· · ·

I want to be careful here about what I claim to have taken from it, because I was a boy and boys do not process grief the way adults do — or rather, they process it in their bodies before they process it in their minds, and the understanding comes later, sometimes years later, assembled from pieces that didn't connect at the time.

What I knew immediately was this: life was serious. Not grim — serious. The difference matters. Grim means the darkness wins. Serious means the darkness is real, and you had better live as though you know it, because the afternoon when the cloudburst comes and the equipment needs collecting is an afternoon that looks exactly like every other one.

What the journal says

"The affect it had on these young men was remarkable. Life suddenly took a much more serious vein for us all."

Decker Noonan was not a member of our church. He had no particular claim on our grief beyond the one that any decent human being has on the people who knew him. But that claim was real, and we felt it, and something in us shifted.

"He was full of industry and jovial and of a happy, contented nature. That is what I remember most. Not what happened to him. Who he was."

I have thought about Decker Noonan many times in the seventy-odd years since that fall afternoon. Not with grief exactly — more with a kind of attention. He was a seventh grader sent out to do a job, and he did it, and the lightning came. That is the whole story. It does not resolve into a lesson. It does not have a moral that makes the fact of it easier to hold.

What it has is weight. And I have carried that weight, lightly but steadily, since I was eleven years old. It has made me take ordinary afternoons more seriously. It has made me look at the people around me with a little more care. It has reminded me, every time I have needed reminding, that the shape of a life is not always visible from the inside of it — that what looks like an ordinary errand can be the last one, and the people who love you will remember what kind of person you were when you went to do it.

Decker was full of industry, jovial, and of a happy, contented nature.

That is the kind of thing worth being.

A Note for the Grandkids In the fall of 1962, when Grandpa was eleven years old and living in Blanding, a seventh grader named Decker Noonan was sent out onto the high school football field during a cloudburst to collect the team's equipment. A bolt of lightning struck the goalpost, glanced off, and went through his head. He was thrown fifty feet across the field. The whole town turned out to mourn. Decker was not a member of the Church, but he was the kind of boy everyone loved — full of energy and good nature, always useful, always present. Grandpa never forgot him. He says the loss made life feel more serious — not grim, but serious. There's a difference. He has been paying attention to ordinary afternoons ever since.
