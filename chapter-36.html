Part Six: Building a Life  ·  Chapter Thirty-Six

The Christmas Tree

D
Dee Stevens Pleasant Grove, Utah  ·  Early 2000s
✦

There was a Christmas when we had no money. That is not an unusual sentence in the story of this family — we were frequently short, and we had learned over the years to make do, to stretch, to pray, and to trust that the Lord would provide something. He usually did. But this particular Christmas the shortfall was acute enough that Kathy and I went out one evening specifically to look for ideas — not to buy anything, just to see what might be possible — and came home discouraged.

Adam and McKay were still in high school. They were good boys who did not complain about what we didn't have, which made it harder in a way. You can talk yourself into accepting a lean Christmas when the children are very young and don't fully understand. You cannot as easily talk yourself into it when the children are old enough to understand exactly what is happening and are being gracious about it anyway.

We had walked through the stores that evening feeling the particular weight of wanting to give something you cannot give. We came home and stepped through the door.

They had been out looking for ideas. Nothing they could afford.
They came home and stepped through the door.
Tucked into the branches of the Christmas tree were hundred dollar bills.
Ten of them. An angel had come in while they were gone.

Someone had entered our home while we were out and placed ten hundred-dollar bills in the branches of our Christmas tree. We do not know who it was. We have never known. They left no note, no indication of any kind — only the bills in the branches, and the fact of the thing itself.

Kathy and I stood there and looked at it for a moment. Then we knelt down together and gave our thanks.

· · ·

I have thought about that moment many times in the years since — the two of us on our knees in front of a Christmas tree in Pleasant Grove, the boys somewhere in the house, a thousand dollars tucked in the branches that had not been there when we left. I have thought about the person who did it. They chose to remain anonymous, which meant they were not doing it for credit or recognition. They were doing it because they felt prompted to and because they could and because it was Christmas and we were a family that needed help that year.

"We were both humbled to tears as we knelt to give our thanks. Some angel had crept into our home unawares. We never found out who."

That is an extraordinary thing to do for another person. It requires a kind of generosity that goes beyond writing a check — it requires imagination, and attention, and the willingness to act on a prompting without waiting for a more convenient moment or a clearer sign. Someone in our life knew enough about our situation and cared enough about our family to do something about it quietly, on their own, and then disappear.

"The Lord does not always send His tender mercies through dramatic means. Sometimes He sends them through someone who has the keys to your house and the sense to use them."

· · ·

These were the Pleasant Grove years — the years of Grandma Wright at the table, of the kidney transplant, of going back to school, of the High Priest Group and the ward clerk and the family history work. Years that were full to the edges with struggle and grace in roughly equal measure.

The money in the tree was not the only time the Lord sent someone to help during those years. It was, perhaps, the most vivid — the most impossible to explain away as coincidence or good timing. Someone had been watching. Someone had acted. The bills were in the branches when we got home.

What the angel understood

Dee and Kathy had raised eight boys on grocery manager wages and faith. They had moved eleven times. They had carried sick children and missionary sons and a ninety-six-year-old grandmother and each other. They had never stopped tithing, never stopped serving, never stopped showing up. The angel with the hundred-dollar bills understood all of that, or understood enough of it, and decided that this particular Christmas it was their turn to be on the receiving end. They were right.

We never found out who it was. I have my suspicions — there are only a few people who would have had access to our home, and even fewer who would have thought to do something like this. But I have never confirmed it, and at this point I have made peace with not knowing. What I know is that it happened. What I know is that we were seen and provided for at a moment when we needed both things.

I know what a tender mercy feels like by now. I felt it that night on my knees in front of the tree.
