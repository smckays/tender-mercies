The Little Old Lady at the End of the Row

Saratoga Springs, New York · Winter, 1971

There is a poem called Joe Always Hoed to the End of the Row. Our mission mother read it to us once at a zone conference, and something about it got under my skin and stayed there. The idea was simple: no matter how tired you are, no matter how cold, no matter how many doors have been shut in your face that morning — you finish the row. You don't stop in the middle.

My companion Elder Andelin and I had adopted this as a personal rule. Whatever street we started, we finished. Every door. We called it hoeing to the end of the row.

It sounds noble. What it actually meant, in practice, was that on the coldest morning of a Saratoga Springs winter, with our car frozen solid under four feet of snow and temperatures that made the air feel like something you could cut yourself on, we were out there knocking on doors anyway.

Nobody was opening them.

When you tract in upstate New York in deep winter, you learn to keep moving your feet on people's porches so your shoes don't freeze to the wood while you wait. Most people took one look at two young men in suits standing in the snow and decided, quite reasonably, that whatever we were selling was not worth the cold air coming through the door.

We had been out for most of the morning. Nothing. Not a single conversation. The only people who'd let us in at all were a couple of fellows who needed us to stand in their entryway just long enough to get warm, and then shooed us back out into the cold.

· · ·

We were standing at the far end of a long residential street, stamping our feet, seriously considering calling it a day, when I looked up and saw it.

Way down at the very end of the road — past where the street lamps reached, past where any sensible person would keep walking — there was a little white cottage with a white picket fence around it. Snow had drifted so deep against the fence that the gate was half buried. In the carport sat a yellow Volkswagen bug that hadn't been moved in what looked like weeks.

Elder Andelin and I looked at each other.

We both knew what the other one was thinking. We had started this street. The cottage was on this street. That was the rule.

So off we went.

"We had to lean hard into the gate just to get it open. The snow was up past our knees by the time we reached the porch."

We knocked. From somewhere inside we could hear noise — a television — but nobody came. We knocked again. Still nothing. I leaned over the porch railing and peered through the living room window.

There in a wooden rocking chair sat a little elderly woman, maybe eighty years old, watching Roadrunner cartoons. The Road Runner had just done something spectacular and she was cackling her head off, completely oblivious to the two frozen young men on her porch.

We knocked louder.

She turned, squinted, and then shuffled to the door and swung it wide open.

Before I could get a single word of our door approach out, she reached up, grabbed me by the cheek with one small hand, and said:

Saratoga Springs, New York · Deep winter, 1971

“So what took you boys so long?”

She had been waiting. She just hadn’t known for what.

Her name was Sister Davis. She was eighty-two years old, had been living alone in that little cottage for some time, and could not shovel herself out. The yellow Volkswagen hadn't moved because she had nowhere to go and no one to go with.

She told us she had been praying for guidance. She was lonely and she knew she needed something, though she couldn't have told you what. She'd had a dream — two young men in suits would come to her door. She was to listen to whatever they taught her.

What she was like

Every missionary who is faithful deserves to find, at least once, a person like Sister Davis. She wanted us there every single day. I think we taught her all six discussions in a week. She asked questions like she was trying to drink the whole river at once. Her eyes were bright and hungry in a way that made you feel what you were saying actually mattered. She had the sweetest little accent I have ever heard. We would find ourselves chuckling at the way she said things, and she would reach up and grab our cheeks and scold us: "Don't you laugh at me, young man."

After those first few visits, we shoveled her out. The yellow bug emerged. From that day on, Sister Davis drove herself to church — twice every Sunday — without missing a single week.

When she was baptized, it was still deep winter and the sisters were worried she might catch pneumonia getting out of the font. They brought their hair dryers to the baptism and crowded around her as she changed, warming her up. Everyone in both wards came to know Sister Davis. She had that quality — the kind of new convert whose joy makes everyone around them remember why they believed in the first place.

As I write this, I am weeping. How we loved this faithful woman. From the moment she heard the gospel message a fire burned within her that gave her new life.

· · ·

I was transferred out some months later. I didn't learn what happened next until years afterward — long after my mission was finished, long after I had come home, married, raised children, moved across half the country and back again.

Twenty years later · Smith’s grocery store · St. George, Utah

A young man came in wearing a missionary tag from that same area of New York. I told him about my service there. He told me that when Sister Davis died, she had left all her money to the Church — every bit of it. Enough to build a chapel in Saratoga Springs. No more thirty-mile drives for the faithful saints in Ballston and Saratoga. A chapel, right there in their own town — because a lonely eighty-two-year-old woman in a yellow Volkswagen bug had been waiting in her rocking chair for two young men who almost didn't knock on her door.

I have thought about that cottage at the end of the street more times than I can count. About what would have happened if Elder Andelin and I had turned back that morning. If we had been reasonable. If we had decided the cold was too much, the morning too fruitless, the walk too long.

Sister Davis would have stayed in her rocking chair. The cottage would have stayed buried. The yellow bug would never have moved again. And somewhere in Saratoga Springs, the saints would still be driving thirty miles to worship.

"The Lord knew about that little white cottage at the end of the row. He had already sent a dream ahead of us. All He needed was two cold missionaries who were willing to keep walking."

That mantra — hoe to the end of the row — has stayed with me throughout my life. I have always felt a deep need, if I start a job or a project, to finish it. Sometimes it has led to great anxiety and frustration when I did not see a clear path. But it stayed with me just the same.

I think that is how most of the Lord's tender mercies work. They are already prepared. They are already waiting. The only question is whether we will hoe to the end of the row to find them.

A Note for the Grandkids

This happened during Grandpa's mission in upstate New York, the winter of 1971. He was about 20 years old. He never forgot Sister Davis — not her laugh, not the way she grabbed his cheek, not the cackling at the Roadrunner cartoons. He found out about the chapel decades later, from a stranger in a grocery store in St. George. The Lord had been keeping that story safe until Grandpa was ready to hear the end of it. "Hoe to the end of the row" became one of the guiding rules of his life.
