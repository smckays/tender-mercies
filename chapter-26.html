Part Five: Letting Go  ·  Chapter Twenty-Six

My Miracle Kidney

D
Dee Stevens Pleasant Grove, Utah  ·  1997–2025
✦

I want to tell you about my kidneys — both of them — because they are part of the story and because the story of how I have lived without them for twenty-five years is one of the clearest illustrations I have of the Lord keeping a specific promise to a specific person.

It started with polycystic kidney disease. I did not know I had it for a long time. The disease is hereditary — cysts grow on the kidneys over years and decades, slowly crowding out functioning tissue until the kidneys fail. My mother's side of the family carried it. It was, as I understood it, simply my turn.

My kidneys began failing in my late forties. By the mid-1990s it was clear they were not going to last much longer. The question became what to do when they gave out entirely.

1997
Both kidneys removed. Dee begins dialysis — four hours every other day — at a center in Pleasant Grove. He quits working. The wait for a transplant begins.
1998–99
Listed for kidney transplant. The wait list is long. There is no guarantee. The dialysis regimen continues.
2000
A matching kidney becomes available. Dee receives his transplant at the University of Utah Hospital. The kidney functions immediately.
2025
Twenty-five years later, the transplanted kidney is still functioning. Dee is in his mid-seventies. His doctors remain amazed.
· · ·

I want to tell you what dialysis was actually like, because I don't think people who haven't been through it have any real idea.

Every other day, for four hours, I would sit attached to a mechanical kidney while it circulated my blood through filters — seven times over — removing the waste my own body could no longer remove. Without it I would have died. With it I lived, but the living was its own kind of hard. I was forced to quit working. I had known for years that this day was coming — I had watched it approach the way you watch a storm build over the mountains — but knowing it was coming did not make it easier when it arrived. It was a miserable life for about a year and a half.

What a year and a half on dialysis looked like
Four hours every other day. Never missed.
Blood filtered through a machine seven times each session.
No longer able to work. Waiting. Praying. Believing the kidney would come.

I prayed specifically and consistently that a kidney would come. Not a vague hope but a genuine petition — the kind I had learned to pray since I was a boy kneeling in Grandma Stevens' house asking for a mission call to Cumorah. I had learned by then that specific prayers get specific answers, and I had learned to trust the Lord's timing even when it was not my own.

· · ·

One morning, about a year and a half into the dialysis regimen, the call came from the University Hospital. They had a transplant for me.

A twenty-year-old young man had been traveling into Idaho in the middle of the night and rolled his car. His death was sudden and untimely. But he had the foresight — at twenty years old — to check the donor box on his driver's license. Because of that small decision, not just I but several other people received the gift of his organs. I have thought about that many times. What an extraordinary lesson in how even our smallest decisions ripple outward into lives we will never see.

His kidney was a perfect match for me. The surgery was performed at the University of Utah Hospital.

The kidney functioned immediately.

University of Utah Hospital  ·  2000
"The kidney worked immediately. I felt the difference right away. Twenty-five years later it is still working. My doctors are amazed. I am not amazed. I expected it to work. I had been promised it would."
· · ·

Not every transplanted kidney does that. Some take days or weeks to begin working. Mine worked from the first moment, as if it had always been there, as if it belonged.

But there was still something I had to work through. Before my transplant, two or three members of my extended family had received transplants and died within two weeks — complications from pneumonia during recovery. I was conscious of this as I tried to heal at home. The fear was real and specific, and eventually it was enough that I asked for a blessing.

Our neighborhood in Pleasant Grove was full of elderly high priests — good men who had lived long and faithful lives. We called two of them. They came to our home one evening, laid their hands on my head, and blessed me to heal.

Pleasant Grove, Utah  ·  2000
"I got up from my chair and have never looked back."

I have thought carefully about the word miracle and whether it applies here. A transplant is a medical procedure. Doctors performed it. Science made it possible. The kidney came from another person — someone who died so that others could live, an act of generosity so profound it is almost impossible to speak of adequately.

And yet.

The matching. The timing. The immediate function. The surgeon told me at the time that transplants were lasting approximately eight years. Twenty-five years later I am still here, still healthy, still running on that borrowed kidney. The specific answer to a specific prayer, arriving in the Lord's time rather than mine, with results that exceed what medicine alone would have predicted.

"I have had both my kidneys removed and survived on a borrowed one for twenty-five years. I do not call that lucky. I call it watched over. There is a difference."

I think about the young man whose kidney I carry. I don't know his name. But I know he was twenty years old. I know he was traveling at night. I know he had already made the decision — long before that night — that if something happened to him, his body would be used to help someone else. He could not have known it would be me. But it was.

I have prayed for his family regularly since 2000. I don't know their names either. But I know what they gave me in the worst moment of their lives, and I have tried to live in a way that honors it — to make the twenty-five years mean something, to be worth the gift.

What those twenty-five years contained

Kathy's illness and death. The marriages of his sons. The arrival of grandchildren. Years of family history work. Teaching. Serving. Moving to Orem. Living alone after Kathy was gone. Writing this book. All of it on a borrowed kidney, still working, in a man who was told it might last eight years. The Lord had things for him yet to do. The kidney was the means He used to keep him available for them.

I am seventy-four years old as I write this. The kidney is still working. My doctors remain careful and watchful, as they should be — it has been twenty-five years and the body changes. But it is working today, which is all I have ever been promised. Today.

That has always been enough. It has had to be. And it has always, so far, been true.
