Part Five: Letting Go  ·  Chapter Twenty-Six

My Miracle Kidney

D
Dee Stevens Pleasant Grove, Utah  ·  1997–2025
✦

I want to tell you about my kidneys — both of them — because they are part of the story and because the story of how I have lived without them for twenty-five years is one of the clearest illustrations I have of the Lord keeping a specific promise to a specific person.

It started with polycystic kidney disease. I did not know I had it for a long time. The disease is hereditary — cysts grow on the kidneys over years and decades, slowly crowding out functioning tissue until the kidneys fail. There is no cure. There is management, and there is time, and eventually there is dialysis or transplant or death.

My kidneys began failing in my late forties. By the mid-1990s it was clear they were not going to last much longer. The question became what to do when they gave out entirely.

1997
Both kidneys removed. Dee begins dialysis three times a week — four hours each session — at a center in Pleasant Grove.
1998
Listed for kidney transplant. The wait list is long. There is no guarantee. He continues dialysis and continues working.
2000
A matching kidney becomes available. Dee receives his transplant at the University of Utah Hospital. The kidney functions immediately.
2025
Twenty-five years later, the transplanted kidney is still functioning. Dee is in his mid-seventies. His doctors remain amazed.
· · ·

I want to tell you what dialysis was actually like, because I don't think people who haven't been through it have any real idea.

Three times a week, every week, without exception. Four hours each session. You sit in a reclining chair while a machine does what your kidneys no longer can — filtering your blood, removing the waste that would otherwise poison you. Without it you die. With it you live, but you live tethered to a machine and a schedule and a clinic, and the living takes a considerable amount out of you.

What three years on dialysis looked like
Three sessions a week. Four hours each. Never missed.
Working full time at the grocery store between sessions.
Kathy driving him, sitting with him, taking him home.
Praying. Waiting. Believing the kidney would come.

Kathy was extraordinary through those years. She sat with me through sessions when she could. She managed everything at home that I couldn't manage. She did not complain — not once, not where I could hear it — and she bore the weight of it alongside me with the same steadiness she brought to everything in our life together.

I prayed specifically and consistently that a kidney would come. Not a vague hope but a genuine petition — the kind I had learned to pray since I was a boy kneeling in Grandma Stevens' house asking for a mission call to Cumorah. I had learned by then that specific prayers get specific answers, and I had learned to trust the Lord's timing even when it was not my own.

· · ·

The call came in 2000. A matching kidney was available. The window for transplant surgery is narrow — you have hours, not days — and we moved fast. The surgery was performed at the University of Utah Hospital.

The kidney functioned immediately.

Not every transplanted kidney does that. Some take days or weeks to begin working, requiring dialysis to continue in the meantime. Mine worked from the first moment it was placed, as if it had always been there, as if it belonged.

University of Utah Hospital  ·  2000
"The kidney worked immediately. I felt the difference right away. Twenty-five years later it is still working. My doctors are amazed. I am not amazed. I expected it to work. I had been promised it would."
· · ·

I have thought carefully about the word miracle and whether it applies here. A transplant is a medical procedure. Doctors performed it. Science made it possible. The kidney came from another person — someone who died so that their organs could give life to others, an act of generosity so profound it is almost impossible to speak of adequately.

And yet.

The matching. The timing. The immediate function. The twenty-five years of continued function in a man who was told he might have five. The specific answer to a specific prayer, arriving in the Lord's time rather than mine, with results that exceed what medicine alone would have predicted.

"I have had both my kidneys removed and survived on a borrowed one for twenty-five years. I do not call that lucky. I call it watched over. There is a difference."

I think about the person whose kidney I carry. I do not know who they were — the protocols of donation keep that private unless both families agree to share. What I know is that someone's family, in the worst moment of their lives, said yes. They gave something irreplaceable to a stranger. That stranger was me.

I have prayed for that family regularly since 2000. I don't know their names. I don't know their faces. But I know what they gave me, and I have tried to live in a way that honors it — to make the twenty-five years mean something, to be worth the gift.

What those twenty-five years contained

Kathy's illness and death. The marriages of his sons. The arrival of grandchildren. Years of family history work. Teaching. Serving. Moving to Orem. Living alone after Kathy was gone. Writing this book. All of it on a borrowed kidney, still working, in a man who was not supposed to still be here. The Lord had things for him yet to do. The kidney was the means He used to keep him available for them.

I am seventy-four years old as I write this. The kidney is still working. My doctors remain careful and monitoring, as they should be — it has been twenty-five years and the body changes. But it is working today, which is all I have ever been promised. Today.

That has always been enough. It has had to be. And it has always, so far, been true.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa has been living without his own kidneys since 1997. Both were removed because of a hereditary disease called polycystic kidney disease. He spent three years on dialysis — four hours, three times a week — before a matching kidney became available in 2000. The transplanted kidney worked immediately and has been working ever since, for twenty-five years. His doctors are amazed. He is not amazed — he prayed specifically for this and expected specifically to receive it. Someone's family said yes when they didn't have to. Grandpa has prayed for them regularly ever since. He does not know their names. He knows what they gave him.
