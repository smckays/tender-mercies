The Gift of a Photographic Memory

Rochester, New York · 1970

Being a missionary in 1970 meant carrying an enormous amount of information in your head at all times. Not notes. Not a phone. Your head. The Church required every elder to memorize six complete missionary discussions — one hundred and ten pages of scripted dialogue — plus one hundred and sixty-two specific scriptures, chapter and verse, to be recalled on demand at any doorstep in upstate New York.

Discussions 6 Complete scripted lessons, memorized word for word. 110 pages total.
Scriptures 162 Specific passages, chapter and verse, recalled on demand.
Books to read 6+ Standard works plus Talmadge, Richards, and others.
Time allowed Weeks New missionaries still memorizing months after arriving.

I was failing at this comprehensively. The stress of my first companionship had locked up whatever memorization ability I might have had. I would read something, set it down, and find it gone. The discussions seemed to have no purchase on my mind. Nothing would stick.

I knew what I needed. I needed help from somewhere my own effort couldn't reach. So I prayed for it — plainly, specifically. Not a general request for things to get better. A specific petition: Lord, I need to be able to memorize. I need my mind to work. Please help me.

· · ·

Almost all at once — not gradually, not after weeks of incremental improvement, but almost all at once — my mind began to work like a trap.

I memorized the entire fourth discussion in one hour. The subsequent discussions fell just as fast. The 162 scriptures became easy to remember — chapter, verse, full text, retrieved on demand without notes. Names and addresses from our tracting I recalled without writing them down. At a special cottage meeting in Schenectady, I was called upon to deliver the main address — twenty-six pages, pre-scripted. I read it through once. An hour later I stood up and delivered it word for word, from memory.

I was not capable of that before my prayer. I am not capable of it now. I was capable of it then, for two years, because I needed to be and I asked to be.

For the rest of my mission I was often able to read a page and remember it word for word. I could recall names and addresses from our tracting without writing them down. I could hold entire conversations in my memory like photographs.
· · ·

Shortly after returning home, the gift drifted away.

No drama. No final moment. Just — gone. The way a tide goes out. Quietly, completely, while you aren't watching.

I was not devastated by this. By then I understood what the gift had been for. It was not a permanent upgrade to my natural abilities. It was a loan — given for the duration of the work, returned when the work was done.

There is a kind of theology in that. The gifts we receive are not always meant to be kept. Some of them are equipment — issued for a specific assignment, returned when the assignment is complete. What matters is not whether you get to keep the gift. What matters is what you do with it while it is in your hands.

I think about this often now, in my seventies, when arthritis has taken my guitar playing from me and age has taken things I once took for granted. The hands that could play John Denver songs for my boys around a campfire can no longer manage the chord changes. That ability was on loan too. What matters is the evenings it made possible — the songs my children grew up knowing, the fire, the voices joining in.

You use what you are given, for as long as you are given it, as fully as you can. That is the whole of it.

A Note for the Grandkids

For two full years on his mission, Grandpa could read a page once and remember it perfectly. He memorized 162 scriptures, six long discussions, and once delivered a 26-page speech from memory after reading it just once. When he came home, the ability quietly disappeared. He always believed it was borrowed — given for a purpose, returned when that purpose was done. He has thought about that loan his whole life.
