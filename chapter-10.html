Part One: The Making of a Young Man  ·  Chapter Ten

The Boy Who Watched the Pageant Three Times in One Night

D
Dee Stevens Schenectady & Palmyra, New York  ·  1971
❧

I have told you about the Hill Cumorah Pageant — about the thirty thousand people on the hillside, the stages the size of city blocks, the lights and the water curtains and the figure of Jesus Christ descending from the night sky. I have told you that the pageant had never been rained out in thirty-seven years, and that I watched an apostle command the rain to stop and it did.

What I haven't told you yet is what the pageant did to people who weren't expecting it to do anything at all.

I want to tell you about Brother Allen.

· · ·

The Allen family came to the elders in an unusual way. The zone leaders in Schenectady knocked on the door and the man inside answered — which was already remarkable, since husbands almost never answered and almost never invited you in. But Brother Allen did both.

The elders got the feeling, early on, that he was meeting with them at least partly to spite his wife. Sister Allen was a very faithful Catholic — attended her masses, kept her sacraments, chided her husband regularly to join the family at church. He was not Catholic and had no intention of becoming one. The Mormon missionaries at his kitchen table were, perhaps, his way of making a point.

But something happened over the weeks that followed. He kept meeting with them. He began to read the Book of Mormon. Slowly, without making a show of it, he became genuinely interested — not to spite anyone, but because the thing itself had gotten hold of him.

Meanwhile, in the kitchen

Sister Allen had noticed what was happening with her husband. A faithful Catholic woman watching her husband study Mormonism was not going to sit quietly. She decided to investigate — to find out exactly what kind of heresy she was dealing with. So whenever the elders came, she would position herself in the kitchen and listen through the door. Week after week, lesson after lesson, she listened from the other side of the wall.

The Spirit, as it turns out, does not require you to be in the room. It passes through kitchen doors just fine.

On the day the elders were going to invite Brother Allen to commit to baptism, something unexpected happened. Sister Allen came walking out of the kitchen. She stood in the doorway and announced, with the certainty of a woman who has made up her mind, that she knew it was all true and wanted to apply for baptism.

The elders found themselves suddenly with two investigators where they had expected one — and the one they had been cultivating for weeks had just been eclipsed by the one who had been listening through the door. All their attention swung to Sister Allen. They could not help it. She was a golden investigator who had materialized out of thin air.

Brother Allen felt the shift. And he did not like it.

He stepped back. He went quiet. The interest that had been building in him for weeks seemed to drain away in the course of a single afternoon. He didn't want anything more to do with the lessons.

Sister Allen and their two children were baptized. Brother Allen came to church with the family each Sunday — he showed up, he sat in the pew, he was present — but he held himself apart. Something had closed in him, and we could not find the way back in.

· · ·

I lost track of the Allen family when I transferred out of Schenectady. But the story found its ending the following summer, during pageant season — and I heard it told by the elders who were still there to witness it.

Brother Allen decided he wanted to see what the pageant was about.

He brought his family to Palmyra on a Monday night. They spread their blankets on the hillside with thirty thousand other people and watched the whole production — the destruction scenes, the water curtains, the music, the Savior descending from the sky at the end.

Brother Allen watched it and said nothing. He drove his family home. They arrived sometime after eleven o'clock at night.

Brother Allen got back in his car.
He drove to his parents' house.
He woke them up.
He told them they had to come with him.
That night.

His parents came. They drove back to Palmyra — the whole way back across the state, in the middle of the night — and they watched the pageant on Tuesday. They stayed through Wednesday. They watched it again on Thursday.

What the Hill Cumorah Pageant did to Brother Allen

Three nights on that hillside. The same production, three times. The music and the water and the light and the Savior coming down from the sky — over and over, until whatever had been resisting in him could not resist anymore.

He drove back to Schenectady. He got baptized. Just like that. After a year of sitting in the pew and holding himself apart, three nights on a hillside in Palmyra undid the whole thing.

I have thought about Brother Allen many times — about what it was, exactly, that the pageant did that the lessons could not do. I don't think it was the spectacle, though the spectacle was considerable. I think it was the cumulative weight of the thing. Watching it once, you could keep your guard up. Watching it twice, the guard begins to tire. Watching it three times in three nights, something in you simply gives way.

"The Spirit is patient in a way that we are not. It will wait. It will come back. It will find the angle that works, even if that angle is a hillside in upstate New York at eleven o'clock at night."

He drove to his parents' house at eleven at night and woke them up because he had seen something he could not keep to himself. That is the mark of a real conversion — not that you feel something privately, but that you feel it so strongly you cannot sit still. You have to get in your car. You have to drive across the state. You have to wake people up.

Sister Allen had been listening through a kitchen door for weeks, waiting for her turn. Brother Allen had been sitting in a pew for a year, just close enough to see but not quite ready to step in. Both of them got there. Just not at the same time and not by the same road.

That seems right to me. That seems true to how this works. The Lord is not in a hurry. He has more patience than any of us, and more approaches than we can count. He can use a kitchen door. He can use a hillside at midnight. He can use a man waking his elderly parents in the middle of the night and telling them to get in the car.

Whatever it takes. However long it takes. He does not give up on people.

A Note for the Grandkids Brother Allen sat in a church pew for a whole year — present but not in, close but not ready. Then one Monday night he watched the Hill Cumorah Pageant and drove home at eleven o'clock and immediately got back in the car to go get his parents. He watched the pageant three times in three nights. Then he got baptized. Grandpa has always loved this story because it's about the Lord refusing to give up on someone — and about what happens when the right thing finally finds the right moment.
