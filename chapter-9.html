Part One: The Making of a Young Man  ·  Chapter Nine

The Family That Almost Wasn't

D
Dee Stevens Rochester, New York  ·  1970–1971
❧

Not every story from my mission ended the way I hoped. I want to tell you one of those, because I think it belongs here alongside the miracles. A life of tender mercies is not a life without disappointment. It is a life in which you learn to hold disappointment in one hand and faith in the other, and keep moving.

The Lancaster family was one of the most remarkable I encountered in my two years in New York. A humble, friendly husband, wife, and teenage son — people you liked immediately, people who made you feel welcome in their home before you had said a thing of substance. We found them through our tracting and they invited us back, again and again.

What was unusual was the father. In our mission, it was almost always the wife who embraced the gospel first. Getting husbands to commit was the great challenge — they would be polite, they would sit through the discussions, but when it came time to make a decision they would find reasons to step back. We had learned not to expect too much from the men.

Brother Lancaster was different. Everything we taught him he received with open hands. He lapped it up and thirsted for more. He asked the right questions. He read. He prayed. You could see it working in him in real time — the way the doctrine was settling into him, making sense, rearranging things.

· · ·

Then we introduced the family to the Book of Mormon.

Brother Lancaster read it the way a man reads something he has been waiting for without knowing he was waiting. He felt the power of it so strongly that one afternoon, when he saw some Jehovah's Witnesses walking past on the sidewalk, he ran out and confronted them — not with hostility, but with the unstoppable energy of a man who has found something true and cannot understand why everyone doesn't already know it. He bore his witness right there on the sidewalk, challenged their beliefs, stood his ground.

What the father was like by this point

He ran to the door every time we knocked. He had his scriptures marked. He had questions ready. His son was right alongside him. The wife was quieter but present, attentive, taking it all in. The Spirit was strong in that home. We had never had an investigator family quite like this one.

The discussions went easily. The baptismal interviews with the zone leaders went smoothly. Half the ward showed up to their baptism date. We were so excited for them we could barely sit still.

The day of the baptism · Rochester, New York

The font was filled. The ward was assembled. The white clothes were laid out. We waited for the Lancaster family to arrive.

They didn't come.

We waited as long as we could. Then we drove to their home, hoping nothing was wrong, trying not to assume the worst.

When we got there, the wife answered the door with her hair in curlers, doing her ironing. Husband and son were nowhere.

· · ·

The story she told us stopped us cold.

She and her husband had been living together for twenty years. They had never been married.

We hadn't known. It had never come up, or we had assumed, or we had simply missed it. And now, on the day of their baptism, the thing that had been quietly sitting beneath the surface had risen up and become the only thing that mattered.

We called the bishop. He was gracious and practical — he explained that as long as they had been faithful to each other, he could come to their home and perform a marriage himself. We could have them baptized the same day. It was not an obstacle. It was a morning's errand.

We relayed this to the wife with what we hoped was the right combination of warmth and encouragement. We expected — I think we genuinely expected — that she would be relieved. That she would call her husband home. That the morning would be saved.

What she said instead
"After living with him for twenty years, I don't think I want to marry him."
Twenty years. The morning she had been working toward without knowing it. And this was the answer.

It blew our minds. There is no other way to say it. We stood in that doorway, in that ordinary afternoon, and we had no answer for what she had just told us.

Brother Lancaster — the man who had run to challenge the Jehovah's Witnesses, who had read the Book of Mormon cover to cover, who had felt the Spirit move in him so powerfully that the whole ward had come to watch him be baptized — lost his faith. Not immediately. But the hurt of that morning went deep, and he could not find his way back to where he had been.

"The gospel asks everything of us. Sometimes what it asks for is the one thing we had not accounted for — the old agreement, the comfortable arrangement, the thing we had stopped examining years ago."

I have thought about that family many times. About what the wife was carrying that she hadn't been able to name until the morning everything came to a head. About what it costs a person to finally say the true thing, even when the true thing undoes everything around it.

I don't have a tidy ending for this one. The Lancasters didn't get baptized that day. I don't know what became of them afterward. I left Rochester not long after, and that chapter closed without a resolution I could point to.

What I kept was this: the work is real, the Spirit is real, the doctrine is true — and human hearts are complicated in ways that have nothing to do with any of that. A person can feel the gospel burning in their bones and still have something in their life that stops them at the door. That is not a failure of the gospel. It is the complexity of being a person.

We planted. We testified. We loved that family as best we could. What happened after was between them and the Lord, and I have faith that it did not end in that living room with the ironing board and the curlers and the answer none of us saw coming.

A Note for the Grandkids Not every story Grandpa tells from his mission ends with a baptism. This one didn't. A father who ran out to defend the Book of Mormon to strangers on his sidewalk. A family the whole ward came to watch be baptized. And a wife who said, at the last possible moment, something nobody expected. Grandpa has thought about the Lancasters for fifty years. He still has faith that their story isn't finished.
