Before the Mission

D
Dee Stevens From his personal journal  ·  Written November 2, 1978
✦
What follows is drawn directly from a personal journal Dee began on November 2nd, 1978 — the day before his twenty-eighth birthday. He was recording his life from the beginning. The chapters that follow this prologue pick up in 1969, when he was eighteen. These are the years before that.
November 2, 1978

The leaders of the church have commanded us with more and more increasing intensity to make a journal for ourselves, a story of the important facts of our lives as well as the spiritual experiences which we would like our posterity to remember. I have started such projects once or twice before and never followed through. But of late, my conscience has pressed me to start again in this more permanent volume, The Story of My Life.

As of tomorrow, I will reach the ripe old age of 28 years old. I see my life divided into three main segments: from birth to age 19, before my mission; my mission from November 1969 to December 1971; and my life from there to now.

I've discovered there are many things in the first two segments of my life which have slipped my memory, so I won't try to do an expanded account of either, but more like an abridgement — to give the facts and experiences which stand out in my mind as having helped me grow into the man I think I might be today. So, without much more ado, I'll proceed to record from memory the events of my past thus far.

November 3, 1950

I was born on November 3rd, 1950 at Utah Valley Hospital in Provo, Utah County, Utah. I was blessed and named Samuel Douglas Richards, which I must admit is a rather important sounding name. Perhaps, if I would have remained Samuel Douglas Richards I would have become some corrupted, but successful politician.

But, as things went, my literal father found he could not content himself with domestic life alone and began wandering in paths which do not lead to exaltation in God's kingdom. My mother, being a virtuous and righteous woman, had her eyes on higher goals and through much embarrassment, heartbreak, and sorrow, became divorced from Mr. Richards sometime before I was two years of age.

In 1952, Merlin Bayles Stevens was serving a mission in the New England States. He had been a resident of Provo most of his life, residing in the Manavu Ward, where my mother's family also lived. He saw a news clipping from a Provo paper telling about the divorce of the girl he had practically grown up with, and the toddler who was left fatherless. He later told me he had turned to his missionary companion and announced, "I'm going home and marry that girl."

That is what he did. He married my mother and I was legally adopted by him and renamed Dee Merlin Stevens.

The people in the basement of our Provo home called me Sammy and I never figured out why — until I got home from my mission and learned for the first time what my first name had been.

On his father, Merlin Stevens

Merlin Bayles Stevens was the son of Edward Stevens and Velyn Bayles Stevens. This is truly noble heritage. My grandmother Velyn Bayles' father was one of the original Hole in the Rock pioneers, who tracked the barren wastes of Southern Utah from Escalante to Bluff on call from the Prophet in 1879. She was born in a little rock home in Bluff City in 1900. Volumes could and should be written about this family and their hardships and toils in bringing law, order, and the Gospel of Jesus Christ to the desolate San Juan region of this state.

My dad bought a lot in Orem and began building a brick home. I think he did almost everything himself except the cement work and the masonry. He built a mantle for the fireplace and a bookcase next to it, and carved the side of the bookcase into an exact fit against that flagstone rock. He was a tremendous wood carver.

That spring we also got involved in building a cabin at Silver Lake. We had logs hauled in and my dad did almost all the work himself, pioneer style with chinking between the logs. We cooked on an old wood stove and heated with a big rock fireplace. Sleeping quarters were upstairs and an outhouse was off in the trees. To this day the cabin is the family's favorite place.

On becoming an honest man  ·  Age 12, Blanding, Utah

I suppose I could have been termed the "artful dodger" of the 1960s. I had become quite adept at shoplifting merchants. I knew it was wrong, but I didn't think of it expressly as a terrible sin.

But as I sat across the desk from the bishop, he surprised the devil out of me by looking me straight in the eyes and asking, "Do you shoplift, Dee?" Panic struck my very soul. I looked that bishop straight in the eyes and said, "No."

The next few days were a literal hell for me. I had lied to the bishop and to me that was ten times the sin that all my shoplifting activities had been. I vowed at that time never to have to lie to a bishop again. I also vowed, along with that, that my shoplifting career had come to an end.

My cohorts in crime figured I would be good for a week until I got ordained and then back to old times. I did get ordained a deacon on the first Sunday in November, 1962 — and I was true to my promise. I began to establish a reputation for myself as an honest man.

On his father and a mountain trail  ·  The Uintah Mountains, circa 1967

Dad and I didn't get along so well for a couple of years. I'd bring home a report card that had some B's on it and I'd feel okay, because everyone was getting B's. But Dad wanted A's. I felt he didn't appreciate me for me. He always wanted me to be better. So I used this as an excuse to argue with him a lot.

Then came the summer our Explorer troop went into the high Uintahs. We hiked into the Henry's Fork region — about 11 miles to the base of Kings Peak — and camped for a week. My father came along with the Bishopric.

About halfway up the mountain, poor old dad was about 200 yards behind us all. And with 50 or 60 pounds on your back, every step is a great accomplishment. Suddenly I heard Dad calling me from way down there. I yelled back and he said, "Come here for a minute." I looked at the trail — I just couldn't possibly go back that far and make it up again. But I thought he might be hurt, so I trudged back down — pack and all — to where Dad was leaning against a rock.

I stopped and glanced into his eye for a second. He then announced, "A minute's up," and away he walked up the hill.

I could have killed the guy. But somehow my heart was moved to love him as I realized that he was lonely all by himself, and he didn't feel he could ask me to walk with him — so he had to trick me into it. I had to grin, and my stubborn old heart warmed up. We walked the rest of the way together, my dad and I, and we talked and listened to one another for the first time in about 5 years. We were the last ones up the mountain, but I was so proud to be with my father as we arrived. I wouldn't trade those precious few hours for all the fish in Dollar Lake.

Now he's gone. I've wished a hundred times I could walk and talk with him again, if but for only a moment. I've felt I've needed him so.

A Note for the Grandkids Grandpa wrote this journal on November 2nd, 1978 — the day before his twenty-eighth birthday. He was recording his life the way the Church had been asking him to for years. He wrote about being adopted and renamed, about the cabin his father built log by log, about lying to a bishop at twelve and becoming an honest man, and about the mountain walk where his father tricked him into company and they talked for the first time in years. His father died in 1972, when Grandpa had only just come home from his mission. He never had enough time with him. But he wrote it down so you would know.
